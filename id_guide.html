Antibiotic & Infection Guide BUILD #1

Home — Basic ID Approach

Concise inpatient approach: history, source identification, empiric therapy, source control, stewardship.

  • History: travel, exposures (ticks, animals), recent healthcare/antibiotics, devices.
  • Exam: targeted to suspected source; document entry points, devices, neurologic status.
  • Diagnostics: obtain blood cultures (2 sets), urine, wound or respiratory cultures before antibiotics when possible.
  • Empiric therapy: cover likely pathogens given site, severity, and local antibiogram; reassess at 48–72 hrs.
  • Source control: drain abscess, remove infected devices, debride necrotic tissue — essential.

Clinical pearls

  • Sepsis: give broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour of recognition; fluids and source control.
  • Neutropenic fever: immediate anti-pseudomonal IV therapy.
  • Always check renal function before dosing; many agents require adjustment.

Infections by Anatomic Site

  • Sinusitis (bacterial): Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg PO BID ×5–7 days (adult outpatient).
  • Mastoiditis: Ceftriaxone 2 g IV q24h; ENT consult for drainage; 10–14 days.
  • Otitis media: Amoxicillin 500 mg PO q8h ×5–7 days (adjust for pediatrics).
  • CAP (inpatient non-ICU): Ceftriaxone 2 g IV q24h + Azithro 500 mg IV/PO day1 then 250 mg daily ×5–7 d.
  • HAP/VAP: Anti-pseudomonal β-lactam ± MRSA agent; 7 days if good response.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: cover anaerobes (ampicillin-sulbactam or pip-tazo).
  • Uncomplicated cystitis: Nitrofurantoin 100 mg PO BID ×5 days (if susceptible).
  • Pyelonephritis (inpatient): Ceftriaxone 1–2 g IV q24h or Pip-tazo 4.5 g IV q6–8h; 7–14 days depending on severity.
  • Catheter-associated UTI: Remove/replace catheter and treat per culture.
  • Complicated IAI: Pip-tazo 4.5 g IV q6–8h or meropenem 1 g IV q8h; drainage of abscesses; 4–14 days depending on source control.
  • Appendicitis prophylaxis: Single pre-op cefazolin 2 g IV (add metronidazole if contamination).
  • Non-purulent cellulitis: Cefazolin 1–2 g IV q8h (switch to oral cephalexin when improving) — 5–7 days.
  • Purulent SSTI: I&D; TMP-SMX or doxycycline if MRSA suspected.
  • Necrotizing fasciitis: Broad-spectrum (vancomycin + pip-tazo or carbapenem) + urgent surgical debridement.
  • Osteomyelitis: Empiric vancomycin ± ceftriaxone pending cultures; 4–6+ weeks therapy guided by ID.
  • Septic arthritis: Drainage + empiric vancomycin ± GN coverage; 2–4 weeks therapy.

High-Yield Syndromes

Detailed management for common hospitalist infectious disease scenarios.

Modified Duke Criteria (2023 ESC Update)

Definite IE: 2 major, OR 1 major + 3 minor, OR 5 minor criteria

Major Criteria:

  • Blood cultures: Typical organisms (S. aureus, viridans strep, S. gallolyticus, HACEK, Enterococcus) from 2 separate cultures; OR persistently positive cultures 12h apart; OR ≥3 positive cultures over >1h
  • Imaging: Vegetation, abscess, pseudoaneurysm, dehiscence, or new valve regurgitation on echo; OR abnormal activity on 18F-FDG PET/CT (prosthetic) or cardiac CT showing abscess/pseudoaneurysm

Minor Criteria:

  • Predisposition (IVDU, prosthetic valve, prior IE, structural heart disease)
  • Fever ≥38°C
  • Vascular phenomena (emboli, mycotic aneurysm, Janeway lesions, conjunctival hemorrhage)
  • Immunologic phenomena (Osler nodes, Roth spots, glomerulonephritis, positive RF)
  • Microbiologic evidence not meeting major criteria

Surgical Indications

  • Emergent (within 24h): Refractory pulmonary edema, cardiogenic shock from severe AI/MR
  • Urgent (within days): Uncontrolled infection despite abx, abscess, enlarging vegetation, persistent bacteremia >7d, fungal IE
  • Elective: Large vegetation >10mm with embolic event, severe valve dysfunction once stabilized

Dexamethasone

  • Indication: Give BEFORE or WITH first antibiotic dose if bacterial meningitis suspected
  • Dose: Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg IV q6h × 4 days
  • Benefit: Reduces mortality and hearing loss in pneumococcal meningitis; less clear for other organisms
  • Discontinue if: CSF gram stain/culture shows non-pneumococcal organism (some experts continue regardless)

Key Principles

  • SURGICAL EMERGENCY: Mortality increases 7-9% for every hour of delay in debridement
  • Types: Type I (polymicrobial, diabetics/elderly), Type II (Group A Strep or S. aureus monomicrobial)
  • Clinical clues: Pain out of proportion, rapid progression, crepitus, bullae, skin necrosis, systemic toxicity

Empiric Therapy

  • Broad-spectrum: Vancomycin + Piperacillin-tazobactam (or Meropenem) + Clindamycin
  • Clindamycin rationale: Inhibits toxin production (Eagle effect—severe GAS may not respond to beta-lactams alone in high inoculum)
  • Duration: Continue until debridements complete, clinically stable, cultures guide de-escalation

Surgical Principles

  • OR within 12-24 hours of presentation (ideally within 6h)
  • Finger test: If tissue planes dissect easily with blunt finger, confirms diagnosis
  • Repeat debridement every 24-48h until clean margins
  • Consider IVIG for streptococcal toxic shock (2g/kg single dose)

  • Chronic, severe, prior abx: Polymicrobial—add GNR, Pseudomonas, anaerobes

Osteomyelitis Considerations

  • Probe-to-bone test: If sterile probe reaches bone through ulcer → 87% PPV for osteo
  • MRI: Most sensitive/specific imaging; get if probe positive or high clinical suspicion
  • Duration: 4-6 weeks if residual infected bone; may be shorter if complete resection

Amputation Considerations

  • Unreconstructable vascular disease with tissue loss
  • Uncontrolled sepsis despite debridement
  • Non-functional limb even if infection controlled
  • Extensive tissue necrosis beyond salvage
  • Patient preference (especially if prolonged treatment/disability expected)

Antibiotic Principles

  • Staph: Add rifampin (biofilm activity) once wound stable; use with companion drug (quinolone, TMP-SMX)
  • Duration: 6 weeks for hip, 12 weeks for knee (if DAIR); may extend for two-stage
  • Suppressive therapy: May be lifelong if implant retained and high risk of relapse

Definition (NHSN/CDC)

  • Central line in place >2 days AND on day of or day before BSI
  • Blood culture positive for recognized pathogen OR common commensal from ≥2 separate cultures
  • No other apparent source

Common Pathogens

  • Coagulase-negative Staphylococci (most common)
  • S. aureus (MSSA & MRSA)
  • Enterococcus
  • Candida species
  • Gram-negative rods (Klebsiella, E. coli, Pseudomonas, Enterobacter)

Complicated CLABSI (Requires Longer Therapy)

  • Persistent bacteremia >72h despite appropriate therapy and line removal
  • Endocarditis (get TTE, consider TEE especially for S. aureus)
  • Septic thrombophlebitis
  • Osteomyelitis or metastatic infection

Classic Triad (present in <15%)

  • Fever
  • Back pain (most common presenting symptom—95%)
  • Neurologic deficit (late finding)

Risk Factors

  • IVDU, diabetes, immunosuppression, alcoholism
  • Recent spinal procedure or epidural catheter
  • Distant infection (bacteremia, skin/soft tissue)
  • Degenerative spine disease

Diagnosis

  • MRI with gadolinium: Imaging of choice—entire spine if clinical localization unclear
  • Blood cultures: Positive in 60-70%
  • Labs: Elevated WBC, ESR (usually >30), CRP

Microbiology

  • S. aureus: 60-70% (MRSA common)
  • Gram-negative rods: 15% (especially with UTI source)
  • Streptococci: 10%
  • TB: Consider in endemic areas or immunocompromised

Management

  • Empiric: Vancomycin + Ceftriaxone (or Cefepime if Pseudomonas risk)
  • Surgery: Urgent decompression if neurologic deficits or progression, failure of medical therapy, or instability
  • Medical management alone: Consider if no neuro deficits, small abscess, high surgical risk, improving on abx
  • Duration: 6-8 weeks IV (longer if vertebral osteomyelitis)

Prognosis

  • Neurologic outcome correlates with severity and duration of deficits pre-op
  • Paralysis >24-48 hours rarely recovers—emphasizes urgency

Definitions

  • Fever: Single temp ≥38.3°C OR ≥38.0°C sustained for ≥1 hour
  • Neutropenia: ANC <500/µL OR <1000/µL with expected decline to <500 within 48h
  • Profound neutropenia: ANC <100/µL
  • Prolonged neutropenia: Expected >7 days

Add Antifungal If:

  • Persistent fever >4-7 days despite broad-spectrum antibiotics
  • Prolonged neutropenia (>10 days expected)
  • Hematologic malignancy or HSCT recipient
  • Options: Caspofungin, Micafungin, Liposomal Amphotericin B, Voriconazole (if Aspergillus suspected)

Duration

  • Continue until ANC ≥500 AND afebrile for 48h (if source unidentified)
  • If documented infection, complete appropriate course for that infection

Sepsis

  • Obtain cultures (blood ×2, site-specific) before antibiotics if feasible — do not delay critical therapy.
  • Administer broad-spectrum IV antibiotics within 1 hour.
  • Give 30 mL/kg crystalloid bolus for hypotension or lactate ≥4 mmol/L.
  • Start vasopressors (norepinephrine preferred) to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg if hypotension persists.
  • Reassess daily; de-escalate when appropriate.
  • Monitor lactate, urine output, mental status, organ function.
  • Adjust dosing in renal/hepatic failure and ECMO/CRRT settings.

Pathogen-Based Guidance (Quick)

  • MSSA: Anti-staphylococcal penicillins (nafcillin/oxacillin) or cefazolin.
  • MRSA: Vancomycin (AUC-based), alternatives: daptomycin (not for pneumonia), linezolid (pneumonia option).
  • Enterococcus: Ampicillin if susceptible; VRE → linezolid or daptomycin (dose per weight).
  • Enterobacteriaceae: ceftriaxone/cefepime/pip-tazo; escalate to carbapenem for ESBL.
  • Pseudomonas: cefepime/meropenem/pip-tazo; consider aminoglycoside synergy in severe cases.
  • Burkholderia: often multidrug-resistant; use ceftazidime/meropenem/TMP-SMX guided by susceptibilities.
  • Bacteroides: metronidazole with beta-lactam partner, or beta-lactam/beta-lactamase inhibitor; carbapenems for severe disease.
  • Actinomyces: prolonged high-dose penicillin therapy (months).
  • Legionella: fluoroquinolone or macrolide.
  • Chlamydia: doxycycline or macrolide.
  • Nocardia: TMP-SMX (often high-dose), alternatives include linezolid, imipenem guided by susceptibility.

  • Monitoring: Weekly CMV PCR during treatment; toxicities (neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, renal for foscarnet)
  • HSV Reactivation:
    • Mucocutaneous (oral, genital)—can be severe, hemorrhagic, disseminated
    • Esophagitis: Odynophagia, discrete ulcers on EGD
    • Hepatitis: Rare, severe, high mortality
    • Encephalitis: Even in immunocompetent—temporal lobe
  • HSV Treatment:
    • Mucocutaneous: Acyclovir 400mg PO TID or Valacyclovir 1g BID × 7-14 days; IV if severe
    • Disseminated/Visceral: Acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV q8h × 14-21 days
    • Encephalitis: Acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV q8h × 14-21 days
  • VZV Reactivation (Shingles):
    • Dermatomal vesicles on erythematous base; can disseminate in immunocompromised
    • Complications: PHN, HZO (ophthalmic), Ramsay Hunt, meningoencephalitis, VZV vasculopathy
  • VZV Treatment:
    • Localized (immunocompetent): Valacyclovir 1g PO TID × 7 days
    • Immunocompromised or disseminated: Acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV q8h until no new lesions × 48h, then oral to complete 10-14 days
  • Pearl: Adjust acyclovir for renal function; ensure adequate hydration (crystalluria risk)
  • Epidemiology: Environmental NTM (water, soil); disease in HIV (CD4 <50), structural lung disease, bronchiectasis, CF
  • Clinical Syndromes:
    • Disseminated (HIV): Fever, night sweats, weight loss, diarrhea, hepatosplenomegaly, cytopenias
    • Pulmonary (non-HIV): Chronic cough, nodular infiltrates, bronchiectasis (Lady Windermere syndrome—RML/lingula)
  • Diagnosis:
    • Disseminated: Blood culture (isolator/lysis centrifugation), bone marrow biopsy
    • Pulmonary: ATS/IDSA criteria—symptoms + imaging + microbiology (2+ sputum cultures OR 1 BAL OR tissue)
  • Treatment (always multidrug):
    • Core regimen: Azithromycin 250-500mg daily (or Clarithromycin) + Ethambutol 15 mg/kg daily + Rifampin (or Rifabutin for HIV)
    • Severe/disseminated: Add Amikacin or Streptomycin initially
  • Duration: 12 months after culture-negative (pulmonary); lifelong suppression may be needed in HIV
  • Prophylaxis (HIV with CD4 <50): Azithromycin 1200mg weekly (less common now with effective ART)

Fungal Infections

  • Empiric: Echinocandin (micafungin/caspofungin) IV for critically ill.
  • Step-down: Fluconazole for susceptible species after clearance and ID consultation.
  • Duration: 14 days after first negative blood culture if uncomplicated.
  • Voriconazole first-line for invasive aspergillosis (weight-based IV dosing then PO); consider TDM.
  • Liposomal amphotericin B for intolerance or certain species.
  • Induction: liposomal amphotericin B + flucytosine; consolidation/maintenance with fluconazole per guidelines.
  • Consult ID for CNS disease and immunocompromised hosts.
  • Liposomal amphotericin B 5–10 mg/kg IV daily; urgent surgical debridement and control of underlying risk factors (e.g., hyperglycemia).

  • Hepatitis A (HAV): Supportive care only; self-limited. Post-exposure prophylaxis for close contacts: HAV vaccine (age 1-40) or immunoglobulin.
  • Hepatitis B (Acute): Supportive care; 95% of adults clear spontaneously. Antivirals (tenofovir, entecavir) only if severe/protracted course, acute liver failure, or immunocompromised.
  • Hepatitis B (Chronic): Tenofovir disoproxil (TDF) 300 mg daily or Entecavir 0.5 mg daily (1 mg if lamivudine-resistant). Treat if HBV DNA elevated with ALT >2× ULN or fibrosis.
  • Hepatitis C: Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) - specific regimen based on genotype and fibrosis. Common: Sofosbuvir/velpatasvir (Epclusa) 1 tab daily ×12 weeks. Cure rate >95%.
  • Hepatitis D: Coinfection with HBV only; pegylated interferon-alpha is traditional therapy. Bulevirtide (Hepcludex) newly approved.
  • Hepatitis E: Usually self-limited; chronic in immunocompromised - reduce immunosuppression, consider ribavirin.
  • IMCRG Reference: #121 Acute Viral Hepatitis - serologic workup, acute liver failure criteria

Tickborne Infections

  • Early localized (erythema migrans): Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID ×10–21 d.
  • Pregnancy/children <8: Amoxicillin 500 mg PO TID ×14–21 d (weight/age adjust) or cefuroxime.
  • Neurocardiac Lyme: Ceftriaxone 2 g IV q24h ×14–28 d for neurologic or severe cardiac involvement.
  • Empiric: Doxycycline 100 mg PO/IV twice daily ×10–14 d; treat promptly on suspicion regardless of age.
  • Presentation: fever, cytopenias, elevated LFTs; can be severe in immunocompromised.
  • Mild–moderate: Atovaquone 750 mg PO BID + Azithromycin 500 mg PO day1 then 250 mg PO daily ×7–10 d.
  • Severe: Clindamycin + Quinine IV; consider exchange transfusion for high parasitemia or severe hemolysis.
  • Empiric: Doxycycline 100 mg PO/IV BID for adults and children; treat immediately on suspicion.
  • Duration typically 5–14 d depending on clinical course.

Viral Encephalopathies / Arboviral Encephalitis

  • Flavivirus transmitted by Culex mosquitoes; most infections asymptomatic or mild febrile illness.
  • Neuroinvasive disease: meningitis, encephalitis, acute flaccid paralysis — supportive care only (ICU as needed).
  • Diagnosis: CSF/serum IgM, PCR early; consider neuroimaging and EEG for encephalopathy.
  • Alphavirus with high mortality; supportive ICU care, aggressive management of seizures and intracranial pressure.
  • No proven antiviral therapy; early recognition and supportive measures crucial.
  • Flavivirus transmitted by mosquitoes; elderly at higher risk for severe neuroinvasive disease.
  • Treatment supportive; involve neurology/ICU for severe cases.
  • Flavivirus endemic in Asia; severe encephalitis possible in a subset.
  • Prevention: vaccine for travelers/residents in endemic areas; treatment supportive.
  • Once symptomatic, rabies is almost universally fatal; immediate post-exposure prophylaxis (vaccine + RIG) is critical after exposures.
  • If symptomatic, supportive ICU care; consult public health and infectious disease immediately.
  • HSV-1 is the most common cause of sporadic viral encephalitis in adults.
  • Treatment: Acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV q8h (adjust renal) for 14–21 days; start immediately if suspected.
