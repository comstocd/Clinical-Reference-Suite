Antibiotic & Infection Guide

Home — Basic ID Approach

Concise inpatient approach: history, source identification, empiric therapy, source control, stewardship.

  • History: travel, exposures (ticks, animals), recent healthcare/antibiotics, devices.
  • Exam: targeted to suspected source; document entry points, devices, neurologic status.
  • Diagnostics: obtain blood cultures (2 sets), urine, wound or respiratory cultures before antibiotics when possible.
  • Empiric therapy: cover likely pathogens given site, severity, and local antibiogram; reassess at 48–72 hrs.
  • Source control: drain abscess, remove infected devices, debride necrotic tissue — essential.

Clinical pearls

  • Sepsis: give broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour of recognition; fluids and source control.
  • Neutropenic fever: immediate anti-pseudomonal IV therapy.
  • Always check renal function before dosing; many agents require adjustment.

Infections by Anatomic Site

  • Sinusitis (bacterial): Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg PO BID ×5–7 days (adult outpatient).
  • Mastoiditis: Ceftriaxone 2 g IV q24h; ENT consult for drainage; 10–14 days.
  • Otitis media: Amoxicillin 500 mg PO q8h ×5–7 days (adjust for pediatrics).
  • CAP (inpatient non-ICU): Ceftriaxone 2 g IV q24h + Azithro 500 mg IV/PO day1 then 250 mg daily ×5–7 d.
  • HAP/VAP: Anti-pseudomonal β-lactam ± MRSA agent; 7 days if good response.
  • Aspiration pneumonia: cover anaerobes (ampicillin-sulbactam or pip-tazo).
  • Uncomplicated cystitis: Nitrofurantoin 100 mg PO BID ×5 days (if susceptible).
  • Pyelonephritis (inpatient): Ceftriaxone 1–2 g IV q24h or Pip-tazo 4.5 g IV q6–8h; 7–14 days depending on severity.
  • Catheter-associated UTI: Remove/replace catheter and treat per culture.
  • Prostatitis (acute bacterial): Fluoroquinolone or TMP-SMX × 4–6 weeks (requires prostate penetration). See High-Yield Syndromes section for details.
  • Complicated IAI: Pip-tazo 4.5 g IV q6–8h or meropenem 1 g IV q8h; drainage of abscesses; 4–14 days depending on source control.
  • Appendicitis prophylaxis: Single pre-op cefazolin 2 g IV (add metronidazole if contamination).
  • Acute cholangitis: Pip-tazo or ceftriaxone + metronidazole; URGENT biliary drainage (ERCP) required within 24–48 hours. See High-Yield Syndromes section for details.
  • Non-purulent cellulitis: Cefazolin 1–2 g IV q8h (switch to oral cephalexin when improving) — 5–7 days.
  • Purulent SSTI: I&D; TMP-SMX or doxycycline if MRSA suspected.
  • Necrotizing fasciitis: Broad-spectrum (vancomycin + pip-tazo or carbapenem) + urgent surgical debridement.
  • Osteomyelitis: Empiric vancomycin ± ceftriaxone pending cultures; 4–6+ weeks therapy guided by ID.
  • Septic arthritis: Drainage + empiric vancomycin ± GN coverage; 2–4 weeks therapy.

High-Yield Syndromes

Detailed management for common hospitalist infectious disease scenarios.

Modified Duke Criteria (2023 ESC Update)

Definite IE: 2 major, OR 1 major + 3 minor, OR 5 minor criteria

Major Criteria:

  • Blood cultures: Typical organisms (S. aureus, viridans strep, S. gallolyticus, HACEK, Enterococcus) from 2 separate cultures; OR persistently positive cultures 12h apart; OR ≥3 positive cultures over >1h
  • Imaging: Vegetation, abscess, pseudoaneurysm, dehiscence, or new valve regurgitation on echo; OR abnormal activity on 18F-FDG PET/CT (prosthetic) or cardiac CT showing abscess/pseudoaneurysm

Minor Criteria:

  • Predisposition (IVDU, prosthetic valve, prior IE, structural heart disease)
  • Fever ≥38°C
  • Vascular phenomena (emboli, mycotic aneurysm, Janeway lesions, conjunctival hemorrhage)
  • Immunologic phenomena (Osler nodes, Roth spots, glomerulonephritis, positive RF)
  • Microbiologic evidence not meeting major criteria

Dexamethasone

  • Indication: Give BEFORE or WITH first antibiotic dose if bacterial meningitis suspected
  • Dose: Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg IV q6h × 4 days
  • Benefit: Reduces mortality and hearing loss in pneumococcal meningitis; less clear for other organisms
  • Discontinue if: CSF gram stain/culture shows non-pneumococcal organism (some experts continue regardless)

Empiric Therapy

  • Broad-spectrum: Vancomycin + Piperacillin-tazobactam (or Meropenem) + Clindamycin
  • Clindamycin rationale: Inhibits toxin production (Eagle effect—severe GAS may not respond to beta-lactams alone in high inoculum)
  • Duration: Continue until debridements complete, clinically stable, cultures guide de-escalation

Surgical Principles

  • OR within 12-24 hours of presentation (ideally within 6h)
  • Finger test: If tissue planes dissect easily with blunt finger, confirms diagnosis
  • Repeat debridement every 24-48h until clean margins
  • Consider IVIG for streptococcal toxic shock (2g/kg single dose)

Osteomyelitis Considerations

  • Probe-to-bone test: If sterile probe reaches bone through ulcer → 87% PPV for osteo
  • MRI: Most sensitive/specific imaging; get if probe positive or high clinical suspicion
  • Duration: 4-6 weeks if residual infected bone; may be shorter if complete resection

Amputation Considerations

  • Unreconstructable vascular disease with tissue loss
  • Uncontrolled sepsis despite debridement
  • Non-functional limb even if infection controlled
  • Extensive tissue necrosis beyond salvage
  • Patient preference (especially if prolonged treatment/disability expected)

Complicated CLABSI (Requires Longer Therapy)

  • Persistent bacteremia >72h despite appropriate therapy and line removal
  • Endocarditis (get TTE, consider TEE especially for S. aureus)
  • Septic thrombophlebitis
  • Osteomyelitis or metastatic infection

Classic Triad (present in <15%)

  • Fever
  • Back pain (most common presenting symptom—95%)
  • Neurologic deficit (late finding)

Risk Factors

  • IVDU, diabetes, immunosuppression, alcoholism
  • Recent spinal procedure or epidural catheter
  • Distant infection (bacteremia, skin/soft tissue)
  • Degenerative spine disease

Diagnosis

  • MRI with gadolinium: Imaging of choice—entire spine if clinical localization unclear
  • Blood cultures: Positive in 60-70%
  • Labs: Elevated WBC, ESR (usually >30), CRP

Microbiology

  • S. aureus: 60-70% (MRSA common)
  • Gram-negative rods: 15% (especially with UTI source)
  • Streptococci: 10%
  • TB: Consider in endemic areas or immunocompromised

Management

  • Empiric: Vancomycin + Ceftriaxone (or Cefepime if Pseudomonas risk)
  • Surgery: Urgent decompression if neurologic deficits or progression, failure of medical therapy, or instability
  • Medical management alone: Consider if no neuro deficits, small abscess, high surgical risk, improving on abx
  • Duration: 6-8 weeks IV (longer if vertebral osteomyelitis)

Prognosis

  • Neurologic outcome correlates with severity and duration of deficits pre-op
  • Paralysis >24-48 hours rarely recovers—emphasizes urgency

Definitions

  • Fever: Single temp ≥38.3°C OR ≥38.0°C sustained for ≥1 hour
  • Neutropenia: ANC <500/µL OR <1000/µL with expected decline to <500 within 48h
  • Profound neutropenia: ANC <100/µL
  • Prolonged neutropenia: Expected >7 days

Source Control & Surgical Indications

  • Surgical drainage indicated if: Abscess >2.5 cm, mass effect/neurologic deterioration, or no improvement after 2 weeks of antibiotics
  • Aspiration vs excision: Stereotactic aspiration preferred (less invasive); excision if multiloculated or failed aspiration

  • LP is CONTRAINDICATED in brain abscess (risk of herniation)
  • Diagnosis: MRI with contrast (ring-enhancing lesion with restricted diffusion on DWI)
  • CT acceptable if MRI unavailable, but MRI is superior for detection and characterization
  • Steroids: Controversial; only for significant mass effect causing herniation risk (may impair antibiotic penetration)

🚨 MANAGEMENT PRINCIPLE: Complete Device Removal Required

CRITICAL: Complete device removal (generator AND all leads) is REQUIRED for all CIED infections (pocket or lead). Antibiotics alone are insufficient and associated with high failure rates (>80% relapse). Partial removal or antibiotics without extraction is NOT acceptable management.

Management Steps

  1. Blood cultures × 2 (before antibiotics)
  2. TEE (evaluate for lead vegetation, valve involvement)
  3. Start empiric antibiotics: Vancomycin 15-20mg/kg IV q8-12h + Gentamicin 1mg/kg IV q8h (until cultures available)
  4. Plan for complete device extraction — Coordinate with EP cardiology or CT surgery
    • Temporary pacing if pacemaker-dependent
    • Extraction (percutaneous lead extraction preferred if available; open extraction if complex)
    • Re-implantation on contralateral side after adequate treatment (typically 2-4 weeks for pocket, 4-6 weeks for lead IE)
  5. Tailor antibiotics based on cultures (typically 2 weeks post-extraction for pocket, 4-6 weeks for lead IE)

🚨 SOURCE CONTROL IS ESSENTIAL — Biliary Drainage Required

CRITICAL: Antibiotics alone will FAIL without biliary drainage. Obstruction must be relieved.

ERCP timing:
• Severe/Reynolds' pentad: URGENT within 24 hours
• Moderate: Within 24-48 hours
• Mild: Within 48-72 hours

Alternatives if ERCP unavailable/failed: Percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography (PTC), surgical drainage

Antibiotic Duration

  • After adequate biliary drainage: 4-7 days total
  • If rapid defervescence + source control: Can consider 4 days
  • If delayed drainage or persistent fever: 7 days
  • If bacteremia documented: 7 days minimum from first negative blood culture

Chronic Bacterial Prostatitis

Diagnosis: Recurrent UTI in men with same organism (E. coli most common)
Presentation: Less acute than acute prostatitis; perineal discomfort, dysuria, recurrent UTI
Treatment: 6-12 weeks of fluoroquinolone or TMP-SMX (much longer than acute)
Note: This is a common cause of relapsing UTI in men — think of it if same bug keeps coming back

Sepsis

  • Obtain cultures (blood ×2, site-specific) before antibiotics if feasible — do not delay critical therapy.
  • Administer broad-spectrum IV antibiotics within 1 hour.
  • Give 30 mL/kg crystalloid bolus for hypotension or lactate ≥4 mmol/L.
  • Start vasopressors (norepinephrine preferred) to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg if hypotension persists.
  • Reassess daily; de-escalate when appropriate.
  • Monitor lactate, urine output, mental status, organ function.
  • Adjust dosing in renal/hepatic failure and ECMO/CRRT settings.

Pathogen-Based Guidance (Quick)

  • MSSA: Anti-staphylococcal penicillins (nafcillin/oxacillin) or cefazolin.
  • MRSA: Vancomycin (AUC-based), alternatives: daptomycin (not for pneumonia), linezolid (pneumonia option).
  • Enterococcus: Ampicillin if susceptible; VRE → linezolid or daptomycin (dose per weight).
  • Enterobacteriaceae: ceftriaxone/cefepime/pip-tazo; escalate to carbapenem for ESBL.

Step 1: Characterize the Reaction

  • What happened? (specific symptoms)
  • How soon after taking the drug? (minutes vs. days)
  • Did they need treatment? (epinephrine, hospitalization)
  • How long ago? (>10 years, IgE wanes)
  • Have they tolerated penicillins or cephalosporins since?

Step 3: Alternatives When Avoiding Beta-Lactams

  • Gram-positive coverage: Vancomycin, daptomycin, linezolid
  • Gram-negative coverage: Fluoroquinolones, aminoglycosides, aztreonam (NO cross-reactivity with penicillins)
  • Anaerobic coverage: Metronidazole, clindamycin

Penicillin Skin Testing

  • Indication: High-risk allergy history when penicillin is strongly preferred (e.g., syphilis in pregnancy, enterococcal endocarditis)
  • NPV: >95% — negative test means <1% chance of IgE-mediated reaction
  • Process: Prick test followed by intradermal testing with penicillin G, penicilloyl-polylysine (Pre-Pen), and minor determinant mixture
  • If negative: Can give penicillins safely (consider supervised first dose)

Desensitization

  • Indication: TRUE IgE-mediated allergy AND no alternative antibiotic acceptable
  • Process: Incremental doses given over 4-12 hours in monitored setting (ICU or allergy clinic)
  • Duration: Desensitization is temporary — must maintain continuous drug exposure. If stopped >24-48h, must repeat desensitization.
  • Contraindicated: SJS/TEN, DRESS, other severe non-IgE reactions

Time to Positivity

Clinical Pearl: Faster time to positivity generally correlates with higher bacterial burden and more severe infection. Cultures positive in <12 hours suggest high-grade bacteremia.

Culture-Negative Endocarditis Organisms (HACEK and Beyond)

  • HACEK organisms: Haemophilus, Aggregatibacter, Cardiobacterium, Eikenella, Kingella — slow-growing, cause culture-negative endocarditis
  • Coxiella burnetii (Q fever): Requires serology
  • Bartonella: Serology and PCR; associated with cat exposure, homelessness
  • Tropheryma whipplei: Whipple disease; PCR for diagnosis
  • Brucella: Prolonged incubation needed; notify lab if suspected

When to Suspect Anaerobic Bacteremia

  • Intra-abdominal source (especially with polymicrobial cultures)
  • Female genital tract infections
  • Aspiration pneumonia with abscess
  • Decubitus ulcers or diabetic foot infections
  • Common anaerobes: Bacteroides fragilis, Clostridium species, Fusobacterium

Persistent Bacteremia — Red Flag

If cultures remain positive >48-72 hours despite appropriate antibiotics:

  • Endocarditis (get TEE)
  • Undrained abscess (get imaging)
  • Infected hardware/device (may need removal)
  • Wrong antibiotic (check susceptibilities)
  • Septic thrombophlebitis (consider anticoagulation)

Antibiogram Interpretation & Use

Local resistance patterns guide empiric therapy. The antibiogram is your hospital's susceptibility report card.

Definition

An antibiogram is a summary of antimicrobial susceptibility test results for organisms isolated at a specific institution over a defined period (usually 1 year). It shows the percentage of isolates susceptible to each antibiotic tested.

Why It Matters

  • Resistance patterns vary significantly by hospital, unit, and region
  • National guidelines provide general recommendations; local antibiogram provides YOUR hospital's data
  • Empiric therapy should target >80-90% susceptibility for serious infections

Limitations

  • Reflects past patterns (typically 1 year old data)
  • Aggregates all isolates — doesn't distinguish ICU vs. floor, inpatient vs. outpatient
  • May not reflect individual patient's prior cultures or risk factors
  • Only includes tested isolates (selection bias)

Interpreting the Numbers

  • >90%: Excellent choice for empiric therapy
  • 80-90%: Acceptable for non-severe infections; consider alternatives for severe infections
  • <80%: Generally avoid for empiric therapy; may use if culture-directed

Scenario 1: Uncomplicated UTI

Your antibiogram shows E. coli susceptibility: TMP-SMX 75%, Ciprofloxacin 72%, Nitrofurantoin 95%

  • Best empiric choice: Nitrofurantoin (95% susceptibility)
  • Avoid TMP-SMX and ciprofloxacin empirically (<80%)
  • This is why IDSA now recommends nitrofurantoin first-line for uncomplicated cystitis

Scenario 2: Hospital-Acquired Pneumonia

High-risk patient, your antibiogram shows: P. aeruginosa susceptibility to pip-tazo 85%, cefepime 87%, meropenem 95%

  • For severe infection, want >90% coverage
  • Best empiric choice: Meropenem (or combination therapy with pip-tazo + aminoglycoside for double coverage)

Scenario 3: Skin/Soft Tissue Infection

Your antibiogram shows MRSA prevalence 35% of all S. aureus isolates

  • If MRSA prevalence >10-20%, consider empiric MRSA coverage (vancomycin, doxycycline, TMP-SMX) for purulent SSTIs
  • If MRSA prevalence <10%, empiric MSSA coverage (cephalexin, dicloxacillin) may be reasonable

General Principles

  • Match severity to susceptibility threshold: Severe infections need >90% coverage; mild infections may accept lower
  • Consider individual risk factors: Recent antibiotics, prior cultures, healthcare exposure may shift probability toward resistance
  • De-escalate when cultures return: Antibiogram guides empiric therapy; definitive therapy is culture-directed

ESBL (Extended-Spectrum Beta-Lactamase) Producers

  • Organisms: E. coli, Klebsiella, Proteus
  • Resistance: All penicillins and cephalosporins (including 3rd/4th gen), aztreonam
  • Treatment: Carbapenems (meropenem, ertapenem); pip-tazo may work for UTI with low inoculum
  • Clue on antibiogram: Susceptible to cefepime in vitro but cephalosporin failure clinically. Some labs report "ESBL screen positive."

AmpC Producers

  • Organisms: "SPICE" — Serratia, Providencia, Indole-positive Proteus, Citrobacter, Enterobacter
  • Risk: May appear susceptible to 3rd-gen cephalosporins but develop resistance during therapy
  • Treatment: Cefepime (stable to AmpC), carbapenems, fluoroquinolones if susceptible

CRE (Carbapenem-Resistant Enterobacteriaceae)

  • Mechanisms: KPC (most common in US), NDM, OXA-48
  • Treatment: Ceftazidime-avibactam (KPC), meropenem-vaborbactam, combination therapy; ID consult recommended

MRSA

  • Check your antibiogram for MRSA prevalence (% of S. aureus that is MRSA)
  • Also check susceptibility to clindamycin (D-test for inducible resistance), TMP-SMX, doxycycline

VRE (Vancomycin-Resistant Enterococcus)

  • Usually E. faecium (E. faecalis resistance less common)
  • Treatment: Linezolid, daptomycin (not for pulmonary infections)

Why Stratification Matters

A single hospital antibiogram aggregates very different patient populations. Resistance patterns differ significantly between:

  • ICU vs. general medical floors
  • Inpatient vs. outpatient (ED, clinic)
  • Different specimen types (blood vs. urine vs. respiratory)
  • Community-onset vs. hospital-onset infections

Outpatient Parenteral Antimicrobial Therapy (OPAT)

Criteria for safe discharge on IV antibiotics, monitoring, and common regimens. Cross-reference: Duration of Therapy, Renal Dosing

Appropriate Candidates

  • Infection requiring IV antibiotics that cannot be treated with oral alternatives
  • Clinically stable (afebrile, hemodynamically stable, no acute decompensation)
  • Reliable IV access (PICC line, midline, or port)
  • Able to manage home infusion or has caregiver support
  • Access to OPAT services (infusion center, home health, or self-administration)
  • Reliable follow-up and monitoring capability

Relative Contraindications

  • Active IV drug use (high risk of line misuse) — consider supervised infusion or oral alternatives
  • Unstable housing or lack of refrigeration (for some drugs)
  • Cognitive impairment without reliable caregiver
  • History of severe allergic reaction or line complications
  • Ongoing diagnostic uncertainty

Before Discharge Checklist

  • □ Source control achieved (if applicable)
  • □ PICC/Midline placed and functioning
  • □ Culture data available and therapy appropriate
  • □ Duration of therapy defined
  • □ OPAT referral placed (infusion services, home health)
  • □ Labs scheduled (see monitoring below)
  • □ Follow-up appointments (ID clinic, PCP)
  • □ Patient/caregiver education completed
  • □ Drug supply secured (prior authorization if needed)

Oral Alternatives to Consider (Avoid OPAT if Possible)

Per OVIVA trial and other data, oral antibiotics with excellent bioavailability may be equivalent to IV for many infections including osteomyelitis:

  • Fluoroquinolones: Levofloxacin, ciprofloxacin (excellent bioavailability)
  • Linezolid: 100% bioavailability; MRSA coverage
  • TMP-SMX: Good for MRSA, some GNRs
  • Doxycycline: MRSA SSTIs, some respiratory
  • Metronidazole: Anaerobic coverage

Bottom Line: Before committing to OPAT, always ask: "Is there an oral option with equivalent efficacy?"

Clostridioides difficile Infection (CDI)

IDSA/SHEA 2021 Guidelines · ACG 2021 Guidelines

Who to Test

  • Unexplained, new-onset ≥3 loose stools/24h in patients ≥2 years old
  • Do NOT test asymptomatic patients or formed stool — high false-positive rate with NAATs
  • Do NOT perform test of cure after treatment — NAAT remains positive for weeks

  • Consult surgery early for fulminant CDI, especially with peritoneal signs, ileus, or megacolon
  • Total colectomy (with end ileostomy) if perforation, peritonitis, or failing medical therapy — mortality 35–80% even with surgery
  • Diverting loop ileostomy + antegrade colonic lavage with vancomycin — emerging alternative to colectomy in select centers
  • CT abdomen: assess for megacolon (transverse colon >6 cm), pneumatosis, free air
  • Avoid antiperistaltics (loperamide, opioids) — risk of toxic megacolon
  • Contact precautions: Gown + gloves for all patient contact; private room preferred
  • Hand hygiene: Soap and water ONLY (not alcohol gel) — C. difficile spores resistant to alcohol
  • Environmental: Sporicidal disinfectants (10% bleach or EPA-registered sporicidal agents)
  • Duration: Continue contact precautions for duration of stay; some guidelines extend 48h after diarrhea resolves
  • Discontinue offending antibiotics as soon as clinically possible — single most important modifiable risk factor
  • Avoid unnecessary PPIs in hospitalized patients — associated with 1.5–2× increased CDI risk

Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)

AASLD 2021 · European Association for the Study of the Liver (EASL) 2018

Diagnostic Criteria

  • Diagnostic paracentesis required whenever ascites patient is admitted or clinical deterioration occurs (do not delay for INR)
  • SBP: Ascitic fluid PMN ≥250 cells/mm³ regardless of culture result
  • Culture-negative neutrocytic ascites (CNNA): PMN ≥250 + negative culture — treat same as SBP (likely culture sensitivity issue)
  • Bacterascites: Positive culture + PMN <250 — repeat paracentesis in 48h; treat if symptomatic or PMN rises

Common Organisms

  • E. coli (~40%), Klebsiella pneumoniae (~20%), Streptococcus pneumoniae, Enterococcus spp.
  • Polymicrobial or anaerobes → suspect secondary peritonitis (bowel perforation) — CT abdomen; surgical consultation

  • Indication: All SBP patients with: Cr >1 mg/dL, BUN >30 mg/dL, OR bilirubin >4 mg/dL

  • Rationale: Prevents hepatorenal syndrome (HRS) — reduces in-hospital mortality from ~29% to 10%

Meningitis & Encephalitis — Expanded

IDSA 2004/2017 Guidelines · ESCMID 2016 · Infectious Disease Society of America

CT Before LP? (IDSA Criteria)

Do NOT delay LP for CT unless one or more of the following present:

  • Immunocompromised (HIV, transplant, on immunosuppressants)
  • History of CNS disease (mass, stroke, focal infection)
  • New-onset seizure (within 1 week)
  • Papilledema
  • Abnormal level of consciousness
  • Focal neurologic deficit

If any criteria met → CT first → LP if no contraindication (no large mass lesion, no midline shift, no effacement of basal cisterns)

Key principle: Do NOT delay antibiotics waiting for CT or LP. Blood cultures then antibiotics if LP will be delayed >30 min.

Blood Tests Before/With LP

  • Serum procalcitonin (supports bacterial etiology if elevated)
  • CBC, CMP, coagulation studies
  • HIV test, cryptococcal antigen (CrAg) if immunocompromised or CD4 unknown

Viral Meningitis (Aseptic Meningitis)

  • Most common cause: Enteroviruses (coxsackievirus, echovirus, enterovirus D68/A71) — seasonal peak summer/fall
  • Other causes: HSV-2 (recurrent/Mollaret meningitis), VZV (dermatomal rash often present), HIV (acute seroconversion), mumps, arboviruses
  • CSF: Lymphocytosis (early may be PMN), normal glucose, mildly elevated protein
  • Treatment: Supportive for enteroviruses; acyclovir if HSV or VZV suspected (10 mg/kg IV q8h until PCR negative)

Encephalitis — Empiric Approach

  • HSV encephalitis (most common treatable viral encephalitis): Temporal lobe involvement on MRI, temporal slowing on EEG, bloody/xanthochromic CSF, lymphocytic pleocytosis. Treat empirically with acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV q8h × 14–21 days; do NOT wait for PCR result — delay in treatment worsens outcomes
  • Other encephalitis causes: CMV (immunocompromise), EBV, HHV-6 (post-transplant), enterovirus, arboviruses (West Nile, EEE), rabies, autoimmune/paraneoplastic
  • Autoimmune encephalitis (anti-NMDA-R, anti-LGI1, etc.): Subacute behavioral change, movement disorders, seizures, autonomic instability; CSF pleocytosis or normal; MRI FLAIR changes; paraneoplastic workup (teratoma in young women for NMDA-R); treat with IVIG + steroids ± rituximab

Empiric Coverage for Undifferentiated Meningitis/Encephalitis

  • Start empirically: Ceftriaxone 2 g IV q12h + vancomycin + dexamethasone + acyclovir 10 mg/kg IV q8h
  • Add ampicillin 2 g IV q4h if age >50 or immunocompromise (Listeria coverage)
  • De-escalate when etiology identified: if viral → stop antibiotics; if bacterial → tailor to pathogen; if HSV negative at 72h with improving CSF → can discontinue acyclovir
  • Risk factors: HIV/AIDS (CD4 <100), solid organ transplant, hematologic malignancy, prolonged steroids
  • Presentation: Subacute onset (days–weeks), headache, fever, meningismus (may be subtle), altered mental status, cranial nerve palsies; elevated ICP is hallmark
  • Diagnosis: Serum + CSF CrAg (sensitivity >99%), India ink, fungal culture; CSF typically lymphocytic pleocytosis, low glucose, elevated protein

ICP Management (Critical)

  • Measure opening pressure at every LP
  • If OP >25 cmH₂O → therapeutic LP to reduce to <20 cmH₂O or 50% initial pressure
  • Daily LPs until ICP controlled; consider lumbar drain if persistent elevation
  • VP shunt for refractory elevated ICP

  • Risk groups: TB-endemic areas, HIV, young children, immunocompromise

  • Induction (2 months): RIPE (rifampin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, ethambutol)

Bone Biopsy — Critical for Diagnosis and Guiding Therapy
Do not treat without microbiologic diagnosis if at all possible. Empiric therapy without cultures leads to treatment failure, prolonged therapy, and drug toxicity. Hold antibiotics until biopsy if patient is clinically stable.

Clinical Presentation

  • Back/neck pain (most common, present in >90%) — localized, often worse with movement and percussion over spine
  • Fever (in only 50–60% of cases — absence does not exclude diagnosis)

Risk Factors

  • Diabetes mellitus, immunosuppression, malignancy

  • Recent spinal instrumentation or procedure
  • Age >60 (degenerative changes facilitate hematogenous seeding)
  • Urologic procedures (GN bacteremia seeding)

Key Pearls for Hospitalists

Osteomyelitis-Specific Pearls

Laboratory & Workup — What to Order (and What Not To)

  • Do NOT reflexively order: UA/urine culture in catheterized patient without symptoms, blood cultures for isolated low-grade fever (<38.5°C) in stable post-op day 0–2 patient, CXR for fever alone without respiratory symptoms in the first 48h
  • Always order blood cultures ×2 for: temperature >38.9°C, rigors, hemodynamic instability, suspicion of deep infection, fever after day 5
  • CRP/ESR: not useful in early post-op (elevated due to surgery); useful after week 2 for tracking trajectory
  • CBC: leukocytosis common first 48h post-op; trend more important than single value

SSI Management

Superficial SSI
  • Open the wound: Open incision over area of fluctuance or purulence; culture deep tissue (not skin swab); irrigate and pack wound
  • Antibiotics NOT routinely required for simple superficial SSI after wound opening (adequate source control)
  • Add antibiotics if: Systemic signs (fever, leukocytosis), erythema extending >2 cm from wound edge, immunocompromised, hardware beneath wound, or cellulitis not improving with wound opening alone
  • Empiric: Cefazolin IV or cephalexin PO (cover skin flora); add MRSA coverage (TMP-SMX or vancomycin) if MRSA risk factors or no improvement
Deep Incisional SSI
  • Requires surgical exploration — debridement, fascial disruption assessment, culture
  • Wound VAC (negative pressure wound therapy) for large wounds, improving healing and reducing risk of ongoing infection
  • Antibiotics per culture; broader coverage for deep structures (vancomycin + ceftriaxone or pip-tazo pending cultures)
  • Duration: typically 1–2 weeks after adequate source control unless hardware involved
Organ/Space SSI
  • Requires image-guided or surgical drainage — antibiotics alone rarely sufficient
  • CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast for intra-abdominal collections
  • CT-guided percutaneous drainage vs surgical drainage based on collection anatomy and patient stability
  • Anastomotic leak: general surgery consultation urgently — may require diversion, takedown, or fecal peritonitis management
  • Duration: 4–7 days after adequate source control (per STOP-IT trial data for intra-abdominal infections)

General Principles — All Implant Infections

  • Biofilm is the fundamental challenge: bacteria encased in extracellular polymeric matrix on implant surface are protected from host immune defenses and antibiotics; organisms in biofilm exhibit phenotypic tolerance (not resistance) to antibiotics at 100–1000× higher concentrations
  • Rifampin is the most biofilm-active antibiotic for gram-positive organisms (particularly Staphylococcus); always use with companion drug to prevent resistance emergence; never monotherapy
  • Source control decision (retain vs remove hardware) is the most important treatment decision and should involve the implanting surgeon and ID
  • Coagulase-negative Staphylococcus (CoNS) appears low-virulence but causes the majority of indolent implant infections — do not dismiss positive cultures

Vascular Graft Infection

  • Incidence: 1–6% of vascular grafts; mortality 25–75% for aortic graft infection
  • Early (<4 months): S. aureus, gram-negative rods; usually presents as SSI or perigraft fluid
  • Late (>4 months): CoNS, S. epidermidis, MRSA, Pseudomonas; indolent presentation; graft-enteric fistula (presents as GI bleed — herald bleed then massive hemorrhage)
  • Diagnosis: CT angiography (perigraft fluid/gas), tagged WBC scan, PET-CT; blood cultures ×2
  • Graft-enteric fistula: Upper GI bleed + history of aortic graft → EGD to evaluate duodenum (most common site); CT if EGD unrevealing; vascular surgery emergency
  • Treatment: Complete graft excision + in-situ or extra-anatomic reconstruction (if infected field) + antibiotics; for stable patients with limited infection, some success with aggressive débridement + graft preservation + lifelong suppressive antibiotics (ID + vascular surgery decision)
  • Empiric antibiotics: Vancomycin + piperacillin-tazobactam (or carbapenem); culture-directed step-down; duration typically 4–6 weeks minimum post-operatively

Mesh Infection (Abdominal Wall / Hernia Repair)

  • Incidence: 0.5–10% depending on mesh type (biologic < synthetic), wound class, patient factors
  • Early (<30 days): S. aureus, gram-negative rods, polymicrobial; usually presents as SSI around mesh
  • Late (>30 days): CoNS, MRSA; indolent fistula, chronic drainage, mesh extrusion
  • Diagnosis: CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast (perimesh fluid, gas, sinus tract)
  • Treatment: Lightweight synthetic mesh and biologic mesh — débridement + retention sometimes possible with adequate drainage + antibiotics; heavyweight synthetic mesh — usually requires removal; sinus tract to skin → mesh excision typically required
  • Empiric: Vancomycin + pip-tazo for initial presentation; narrow based on cultures; duration 4–6 weeks if mesh retained

Breast Implant Infection

  • Early (<6 weeks): S. aureus, gram-negative rods; treat with antibiotics; implant removal often required if not improving in 48–72h
  • Late: CoNS, atypical mycobacteria (M. abscessus, M. fortuitum — rapidly growing); indolent periprosthetic infection; chronic drainage
  • Atypical mycobacteria: Require specific susceptibility testing; treat with macrolide (clarithromycin) + amikacin ± imipenem based on species; ID consultation required; implant removal usually necessary

Colorectal Surgery — Anastomotic Leak

  • Incidence: 3–10% for colorectal anastomoses; higher for low rectal anastomoses, emergency surgery, malnutrition, steroid use
  • Presentation: usually day 5–7; fever, abdominal pain, leukocytosis, tachycardia, increasing drain output with feculent character
  • Diagnosis: CT abdomen/pelvis with contrast — peranastomotic fluid, gas, extravasation; water-soluble contrast enema if CT equivocal
  • Management: Broad-spectrum antibiotics (pip-tazo or meropenem) + source control; contained leak → CT-guided drainage ± diverting ostomy; free perforation → surgical emergency, wash-out, diversion; anastomotic takedown if necessary
  • Long-term: increased risk of anastomotic stricture and pelvic sepsis

Common Inpatient Prophylaxis Questions

QuestionAnswer
How long should surgical prophylaxis be continued?Single dose or ≤24h for virtually all procedures. No benefit from extending based on drains, catheters, or lines. ASHP/IDSA/SIS/SHEA 2013: strong evidence against prolonged prophylaxis.
Should prophylaxis be continued if the patient has a drain?No. Drains do not extend prophylaxis indication.
Should prophylaxis be continued in the ICU post-cardiac surgery?≤24h only (cefazolin). Some institutions extend to 48h for open-heart surgery — controversial and not guideline-supported; do not extend further.
Patient was given cefazolin but now has positive MRSA screen?Do not add vancomycin retrospectively to prophylaxis. If MRSA screen was positive preoperatively, patient should have received vancomycin + cefazolin prophylaxis.
Penicillin allergy — which alternative for orthopedic/cardiac surgery?Vancomycin 15 mg/kg IV (infuse over ≥60 min to avoid red man syndrome). Cross-reactivity between cephalosporins and penicillin is very low (<2%); consider cefazolin with allergy evaluation if only non-IgE-mediated allergy reported.
Re-dosing during prolonged surgery?Cefazolin: re-dose every 4h (or with blood loss >1.5L); ampicillin-sulbactam: every 2h; vancomycin: not typically re-dosed due to long half-life.
