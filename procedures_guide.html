Paracentesis

Diagnostic and therapeutic removal of ascitic fluid. Most commonly performed for new-onset ascites evaluation or treatment of tense ascites.

Indications

  • Diagnostic: New-onset ascites, suspected SBP (fever, abdominal pain, encephalopathy, renal dysfunction in cirrhotic)
  • Therapeutic: Tense ascites causing respiratory compromise or discomfort
  • ALL hospital admissions with ascites should have diagnostic paracentesis to rule out SBP

Absolute Contraindications

  • Acute abdomen requiring surgery
  • DIC with clinical bleeding

Relative Contraindications (Proceed with Caution)

  • Severe bowel distension (use US guidance)
  • Previous abdominal surgery with adhesions
  • Pregnancy (use US guidance, adjust site)

‚úì Key Point: Do NOT Delay for Coagulopathy

Routine correction of INR or platelets is NOT recommended before paracentesis in cirrhotic patients. Bleeding complications are rare (<1%) and not predicted by lab values.

Site Selection

  • Left lower quadrant (LLQ) preferred: 2-4 cm medial and superior to ASIS
  • Avoid scars, visible vessels, rectus muscles
  • Always use ultrasound to confirm fluid pocket and mark site

Procedure Steps

  1. Position patient supine or slightly lateral decubitus (LLQ down if LLQ approach)
  2. Use ultrasound to identify largest pocket and mark site
  3. Sterile prep and drape; infiltrate with lidocaine (skin ‚Üí peritoneum)
  4. Insert needle perpendicular to skin with "Z-track" technique (pull skin caudally before insertion)
  5. Advance with gentle aspiration until fluid return
  6. For large-volume paracentesis: use 8-French catheter over needle
  7. Remove fluid; send labs from first tube (before contamination)

üí° Z-Track Technique

Pull skin 2 cm caudally before needle insertion, then release after catheter placed. This creates an angled tract that seals when skin retracts, reducing post-procedure leakage.

Large-Volume Paracentesis (>5L)

  • Albumin replacement: Give 6-8 g albumin per liter removed if >5L
  • Example: 8L removed ‚Üí give 50g (2 vials of 25g) albumin
  • Albumin prevents post-paracentesis circulatory dysfunction (PPCD)

Secondary vs Spontaneous Peritonitis

Suspect secondary bacterial peritonitis (perforated viscus) if:

  • Ascites protein >1 g/dL
  • Glucose <50 mg/dL
  • LDH > serum LDH
  • Polymicrobial culture
  • Poor response to antibiotics

Secondary peritonitis requires surgical evaluation, not just antibiotics.

Thoracentesis

Diagnostic and therapeutic removal of pleural fluid. Essential for evaluating new effusions and relieving dyspnea from large effusions.

Indications

  • Diagnostic: Any new clinically significant pleural effusion (>1 cm on lateral decubitus or US)
  • Therapeutic: Large effusion causing dyspnea

Contraindications

  • Minimal fluid (<1 cm on US)
  • Overlying cellulitis
  • Bleeding diathesis (relative ‚Äì use US guidance, may need correction)

‚ö†Ô∏è Anticoagulation Considerations

Consider holding anticoagulation if possible. If cannot hold, use US guidance with experienced operator. Risk of hemothorax is low but consequential.

Site Selection

  • Patient seated, leaning forward over bedside table
  • Use ultrasound to identify largest pocket, measure depth, mark site
  • Enter 1-2 interspaces below fluid level, above the diaphragm
  • Insert over TOP of rib (neurovascular bundle runs under rib)
  • Typically posterior axillary line, 7th-9th intercostal space

Procedure Steps

  1. Position patient seated, arms supported on bedside table
  2. Ultrasound to mark site and measure depth
  3. Sterile prep and drape
  4. Anesthetize skin, then advance along planned trajectory to parietal pleura (will feel "pop")
  5. Insert needle (attached to syringe or catheter system) perpendicular to chest wall, advancing while aspirating
  6. Once fluid returns, advance catheter (if using), remove needle
  7. Aspirate fluid; for large-volume, connect to vacuum bottle
  8. Stop if patient develops chest tightness, cough, or 1.5L removed (whichever first)
  9. Withdraw catheter, apply occlusive dressing

üí° Maximum Volume

Traditional teaching: limit to 1-1.5L per session to prevent re-expansion pulmonary edema (REPE). However, REPE is rare. More important: stop if patient develops chest tightness, cough, or discomfort (suggests lung re-expansion reaching chest wall).

Light's Criteria: Exudate vs Transudate

EXUDATE if meets ANY ONE criterion:

CriterionExudate Threshold
Pleural protein / Serum protein>0.5
Pleural LDH / Serum LDH>0.6
Pleural LDH>2/3 upper limit of normal serum LDH

Sensitivity 98%, Specificity 83% ‚Äî Light's criteria may misclassify transudates as exudates in diuresed CHF patients.

üí° Diuresis Pitfall

If Light's criteria suggest exudate but clinical picture is CHF (bilateral effusions, responding to diuresis), check serum-pleural albumin gradient. If >1.2 g/dL, likely transudate despite Light's criteria (diuresis concentrated the fluid).

Transudate Causes

  • Heart failure (most common)
  • Cirrhosis (hepatic hydrothorax)
  • Nephrotic syndrome
  • Hypoalbuminemia
  • Peritoneal dialysis

Exudate Causes

  • Infection: Pneumonia (parapneumonic), empyema, TB
  • Malignancy: Lung cancer, breast cancer, lymphoma, mesothelioma
  • Inflammatory: PE, pancreatitis, rheumatoid, lupus
  • Other: Post-CABG, Dressler syndrome, chylothorax

Lumbar Puncture

Diagnostic collection of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). Essential for evaluating meningitis, SAH, inflammatory conditions.

Indications

  • Meningitis: Fever + headache + neck stiffness; altered mental status; sepsis without source
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage: Thunderclap headache with negative CT (need LP within 6-12h of symptom onset)
  • Other: CNS malignancy, inflammatory conditions (MS, GBS), normal pressure hydrocephalus (therapeutic)

Absolute Contraindications

  • Evidence of increased ICP with mass effect (risk of herniation)
  • Skin infection over puncture site

When to Get CT Before LP

‚ö†Ô∏è CT Head Before LP If:

  • Immunocompromised
  • History of CNS disease (mass, stroke, infection)
  • New-onset seizure (within 1 week)
  • Papilledema
  • Altered level of consciousness
  • Focal neurologic deficit

If none of these present, can proceed to LP without CT. Do NOT delay antibiotics for CT or LP in suspected bacterial meningitis.

Coagulation Requirements

  • Platelets >50,000 (some say >20,000 for diagnostic, >50,000 if therapeutic anticoagulation)
  • INR <1.5 (or reversal if urgent)
  • Hold DOAC/LMWH per half-life before procedure

Positioning

  • Lateral decubitus (fetal position): Preferred for opening pressure measurement
  • Sitting: Opens up interspace (easier in obese patients); cannot measure OP accurately

Landmark Identification

  • Posterior superior iliac crests ‚Üí line between them crosses L3-L4 or L4-L5 interspace
  • Use L3-L4 or L4-L5 (cord ends at L1-L2 in adults)
  • Ultrasound can help identify midline and interspace in difficult anatomy

Procedure Steps

  1. Position patient lateral decubitus with knees tucked to chest, or seated leaning forward
  2. Identify interspace (L3-L4 or L4-L5) using landmarks ¬± ultrasound
  3. Sterile prep and drape
  4. Anesthetize skin and deeper tissues with lidocaine
  5. Insert spinal needle (20-22G) with stylet in place, bevel parallel to spine (dural fibers run longitudinally)
  6. Advance slowly; may feel "pop" through ligamentum flavum and dura
  7. Remove stylet and check for CSF flow
  8. Attach manometer if measuring opening pressure (normal 6-20 cm H2O; <25 cm H2O)
  9. Collect CSF into numbered tubes (typically 4 tubes, 1-2 mL each)
  10. Replace stylet before removing needle (may reduce post-LP headache)

üí° Atraumatic Needles

Atraumatic (pencil-point) needles like Sprotte or Whitacre reduce post-LP headache compared to cutting needles. Use when available.

Post-LP Headache

  • Onset within 5 days of LP, typically within 48h
  • Positional: worse when upright, improves when supine
  • May be accompanied by neck stiffness, nausea, photophobia, tinnitus

Management

  1. Conservative: Bed rest (supine), hydration, caffeine (300-500 mg), analgesics
  2. If persists >48h or severe: Epidural blood patch (10-20 mL autologous blood). 90%+ effective.

Myth: Bed rest after LP does NOT prevent post-LP headache. Patient can mobilize normally after procedure.

Arthrocentesis

Aspiration of synovial fluid from joints. Essential for evaluating acute monoarticular arthritis (septic joint vs crystal arthropathy).

Indications

  • Diagnostic: Acute monoarticular arthritis (MUST rule out septic arthritis), new joint effusion, suspected crystal arthropathy
  • Therapeutic: Symptomatic effusion, hemarthrosis, steroid injection

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Rule

Every acute hot swollen joint is septic until proven otherwise. Delay in diagnosis leads to joint destruction. Tap it.

Contraindications

  • Cellulitis overlying joint (don't track bacteria in)
  • Prosthetic joint ‚Äì refer to orthopedics (still needs aspiration, but sterile OR technique)
  • Severe coagulopathy (relative ‚Äì bleeding rare from joint tap)

General Technique Principles

  • Use largest gauge needle tolerated (18-20G) to ensure adequate flow
  • Approach from extensor surface when possible (fewer nerves/vessels)
  • Ultrasound guidance improves success, especially small joints

Standard Studies

  • Always: Cell count with differential, Gram stain, culture, crystal analysis
  • If indicated: Glucose (compare to serum), culture for TB/fungal

Septic arthritis can occur with WBC <50,000 (especially partially treated, immunocompromised, or prosthetic joints). Crystal arthropathy can have WBC >50,000. Clinical context matters.

Gout and septic arthritis can coexist. If high clinical suspicion for infection, treat empirically even if crystals seen. Send culture regardless.

Central Line-Associated Bloodstream Infection (CLABSI) Prevention

  • Maximal sterile barriers: Cap, mask, sterile gown and gloves, full-body drape
  • Chlorhexidine skin prep: 2% chlorhexidine with 70% alcohol, allow to dry
  • Avoid femoral site when possible
  • Daily review: "Is this line still needed?" Remove as soon as possible
  • Chlorhexidine-impregnated dressings reduce infection
