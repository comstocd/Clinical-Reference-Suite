Procedures Quick Reference

Evidence-based technique guidance, fluid interpretation, and troubleshooting for 13 core bedside procedures. All sections include indications, contraindications, step-by-step technique, and complications.

📋 Pre-Procedure Checklist (All Procedures)

  • Indication: Document clinical reason in chart
  • Consent: Informed consent obtained and documented
  • Coagulation: Check INR, platelets per procedure-specific thresholds below
  • Anticoagulation: Hold anticoagulants as appropriate (procedure-specific)
  • Timeout: Verify patient identity, procedure, site with team
  • Ultrasound: Use US guidance whenever available — reduces complications across all procedures
  • Allergies: Confirm no allergy to lidocaine (amide class), iodine, latex

Paracentesis

Diagnostic and therapeutic removal of ascitic fluid. All patients admitted with ascites should have a diagnostic paracentesis to rule out SBP.

Indications

  • Diagnostic: New-onset ascites (any cause), suspected SBP (fever, abdominal pain, encephalopathy, or renal dysfunction in cirrhotic patient)
  • Therapeutic: Tense ascites causing respiratory compromise, discomfort, or early satiety
  • Rule: All hospital admissions with known or suspected ascites → diagnostic para on admission

Absolute Contraindications

  • Acute abdomen requiring immediate surgery
  • DIC with active clinical bleeding

Relative Contraindications

  • Severe bowel distension — use ultrasound guidance
  • Prior abdominal surgery with adhesions — use ultrasound guidance
  • Pregnancy — adjust site with ultrasound guidance

✓ Do NOT Delay for Coagulopathy

Routine correction of INR or platelets is NOT recommended before paracentesis in cirrhotic patients (AASLD guideline). Bleeding is rare (<1%) and not predicted by INR or platelet count.

Site Selection

  • Preferred: Left lower quadrant — 2–4 cm medial and superior to ASIS
  • Alternatively: Right LLQ (same landmarks) or midline infraumbilical (linea alba, avascular)
  • Avoid: Scars, visible vessels, rectus abdominis muscle (risk of inferior epigastric artery)
  • Always use ultrasound to confirm fluid pocket depth and absence of bowel at site

Procedure

  1. Position patient supine or slightly lateral decubitus (affected side down to pool fluid)
  2. Use ultrasound to identify largest fluid pocket, measure depth, confirm no bowel; mark skin
  3. Sterile prep and drape; don sterile gloves
  4. Anesthetize with 1% lidocaine: raise skin wheal, then advance slowly anesthetizing subcutaneous tissue and parietal peritoneum (will feel pop and loss of resistance)
  5. Apply Z-track: pull skin 2 cm caudally, then insert 18G needle attached to syringe perpendicular to skin, aspirating continuously
  6. Advance until fluid returns; for diagnostic tap, aspirate 30–60 mL and send labs
  7. For large-volume paracentesis: advance 8-French catheter over needle (Seldinger or trocar technique), remove needle, connect to vacuum drainage system
  8. Send first tube of fluid for cell count BEFORE connecting to vacuum (avoid contamination)
  9. Release Z-track skin traction when catheter secured; drain desired volume
  10. Remove catheter, apply pressure, place occlusive dressing; document output volume

💡 Z-Track Technique

Pull skin 2 cm caudally before needle insertion, then release after catheter placed. Creates an angled tract — when skin returns to position, the tract seals, preventing post-procedure ascitic fluid leak.

Large-Volume Paracentesis (>5L)

  • Albumin replacement: 6–8 g IV albumin per liter removed for volumes >5L
  • Example: 8L drained → 48–64g albumin = 2–3 vials of 25g (50–75g, round up)
  • Albumin prevents post-paracentesis circulatory dysfunction (PPCD) — a major cause of hepatorenal syndrome and death
  • No benefit shown for volumes <5L (EASL guideline)

Thoracentesis

Aspiration of pleural fluid for diagnosis or symptom relief. Mandatory for any new, undiagnosed, clinically significant pleural effusion.

Indications

  • Diagnostic: Any new pleural effusion >1 cm on lateral decubitus film or ultrasound without obvious cause (e.g., bilateral effusions in clear CHF may not need tap)
  • Therapeutic: Symptomatic large effusion causing dyspnea, hypoxia, or chest discomfort
  • Urgent: Suspected empyema (fever + effusion + sepsis) — needs drainage regardless of size

Contraindications

  • Minimal fluid (<1 cm on US — too risky)
  • Overlying cellulitis or skin infection at planned site
  • Uncooperative patient (unable to remain still)
  • Coagulopathy uncorrected: platelets <25,000 or INR >3.0 (relative; weigh risk/benefit)

💡 Bilateral CHF Effusions

Don't need diagnostic thoracentesis if effusions are bilateral, symmetric, and patient has clear CHF diagnosis. Tap if: unilateral, fever, chest pain, fails to respond to diuresis, or clinical suspicion for another cause.

Setup

  • Position: Patient seated upright, leaning forward over bedside table with arms supported
  • If unable to sit: lateral decubitus (affected side up)
  • Use ultrasound to identify largest fluid pocket, measure depth to lung, mark site and level

Site Selection

  • Posterior axillary line, typically 7th–9th intercostal space
  • Enter 1–2 interspaces below the upper fluid level
  • Always enter OVER the TOP of the rib — neurovascular bundle (nerve-artery-vein, top to bottom) runs in the subcostal groove

Procedure

  1. Use ultrasound to mark site; measure skin-to-fluid depth and fluid depth
  2. Sterile prep and drape; position patient seated leaning forward
  3. Anesthetize with 1% lidocaine: skin wheal, then walk needle over rib top, anesthetize subcutaneous tissue, intercostal muscle, and parietal pleura (sharp pain as needle enters pleura → inject 2–3 mL, then withdraw slightly)
  4. Note depth to fluid on lidocaine syringe
  5. Insert thoracentesis needle (or catheter-over-needle) at same trajectory, aspirating continuously until fluid returns
  6. For diagnostic: aspirate 50–60 mL into syringe, remove needle, apply occlusive dressing
  7. For therapeutic: advance catheter, remove needle, connect to three-way stopcock and drainage system
  8. Remove no more than 1–1.5L per session; stop earlier if patient develops cough, chest tightness, or pain (suggests lung re-expansion approaching chest wall)
  9. Remove catheter at end of exhalation; apply occlusive dressing
  10. Post-procedure: CXR only if symptomatic, air aspirated, multiple attempts, or non-US-guided

⚠️ Drainage Limit

Traditional limit of 1–1.5L/session to prevent re-expansion pulmonary edema (REPE). REPE is rare, but stop immediately if patient develops chest tightness, sustained cough, or hemoptysis — these indicate lung has re-expanded to chest wall.

Light's Criteria (Exudate vs. Transudate)

Exudate if ANY ONE criterion met:

  • Pleural protein / Serum protein >0.5
  • Pleural LDH / Serum LDH >0.6
  • Pleural LDH >2/3 upper limit of normal serum LDH

Sensitivity 98%, Specificity 83%. Light's may misclassify diuresed CHF transudates as exudates — if clinical picture suggests CHF, check serum-pleural albumin gradient. If >1.2 g/dL → true transudate despite Light's criteria.

Lumbar Puncture

CSF sampling for diagnosis of meningitis, encephalitis, subarachnoid hemorrhage, and inflammatory CNS disease. Do not delay antibiotics for LP.

Indications

  • Meningitis/encephalitis: Fever + headache + neck stiffness; AMS; suspected CNS infection in sepsis without source
  • Subarachnoid hemorrhage: Thunderclap headache ("worst headache of life") with normal CT — xanthochromia on LP within 6–12h of onset; sensitivity near 100% at 12h
  • Normal pressure hydrocephalus: Gait ataxia + incontinence + dementia triad; LP may be therapeutic
  • Other: GBS (albuminocytologic dissociation), MS (oligoclonal bands), CNS malignancy, IIH (opening pressure), cryptococcal antigen

Absolute Contraindications

  • Evidence of increased ICP with mass effect on CT (midline shift, effaced cisterns, herniation) — risk of tonsillar herniation
  • Skin infection or epidural abscess at puncture site
  • Unstable cardiopulmonary status

Relative Contraindications

  • Coagulopathy: platelets <50,000 or INR >1.5 (correct before LP)
  • Posterior fossa mass or obstructive hydrocephalus — risk of herniation even without supratentorial shift
  • Anticoagulation (see hold times on home page)

🚨 CRITICAL: Do NOT Delay Antibiotics for LP or CT

In suspected bacterial meningitis: start empiric antibiotics and dexamethasone IMMEDIATELY if LP will be delayed >30 minutes. LP within 1–2h of antibiotics still yields positive culture in most cases. Never delay treatment for imaging or LP.

When to Get CT Before LP

CT required before LP if ANY of the following (Hasbun criteria):

  • Age >60
  • Immunocompromised
  • Known CNS disease (mass, stroke, focal infection)
  • Seizure within 1 week
  • Papilledema on fundoscopy
  • Focal neurologic deficit
  • Altered level of consciousness

If none of the above present, proceed to LP without CT.

Positioning

  • Lateral decubitus (fetal position): Preferred — knees to chest, chin tucked. Required for accurate opening pressure measurement. Hips perpendicular to bed.
  • Seated (upright): Easier in obese patients; patient leans forward over pillow; CANNOT accurately measure opening pressure in this position

Landmark Identification

  • Identify L4–L5 or L3–L4 interspace — line connecting iliac crests (Tuffier's line) crosses L4 spinous process
  • Ultrasound guidance significantly improves first-attempt success in obese patients
  • Spinal cord ends at L1–L2; conus medullaris at L1 in adults — always enter L3–L4 or below
  1. Position patient; identify and mark interspace (L3–L4 or L4–L5)
  2. Sterile prep and drape; sterile gloves
  3. Anesthetize with 1% lidocaine: skin wheal, then advance needle anesthetizing down to interspinous ligament
  4. Insert spinal needle (22G atraumatic/Sprotte preferred — lower post-LP headache rate) with bevel parallel to dural fibers (longitudinal in lateral decubitus)
  5. Advance with stylet in place through skin → subcutaneous tissue → supraspinous ligament → interspinous ligament → ligamentum flavum (increased resistance) → epidural space → dura (pop or loss of resistance)
  6. Remove stylet — CSF should flow. If none: rotate needle 90°, confirm patient position, try re-advancing slightly
  7. Attach manometer for opening pressure (patient must be relaxed, legs extended if possible; ask to breathe slowly)
  8. Collect CSF in 4 tubes sequentially: Tube 1 (cell count), Tube 2 (glucose, protein), Tube 3 (Gram stain, culture), Tube 4 (cell count — to assess traumatic tap vs true RBCs)
  9. Replace stylet before withdrawing needle (reduces post-LP headache)
  10. Apply bandage; patient may resume normal activity immediately (bed rest does NOT prevent headache)

Traumatic tap: RBCs decrease from tube 1 → tube 4; fluid clears; no xanthochromia
True SAH: Uniform RBCs in all tubes; xanthochromia present (yellow color from bilirubin, develops 6–12h after bleed); positive visual spectrophotometry

Arthrocentesis

Aspiration of synovial fluid. Every acute hot swollen joint requires arthrocentesis — septic arthritis must be excluded before any other diagnosis is made.

Indications

  • Diagnostic (mandatory): Acute monoarticular arthritis — must rule out septic arthritis
  • Diagnostic: New joint effusion of unknown cause, suspected crystal arthropathy, evaluation for hemarthrosis
  • Therapeutic: Symptomatic tense effusion, hemarthrosis, intra-articular steroid injection

🚨 Critical Rule

Every acute hot swollen joint is septic arthritis until proven otherwise. Delay in diagnosis leads to irreversible cartilage destruction within hours to days. When in doubt, tap it. Crystals on microscopy do NOT exclude concurrent infection.

Contraindications

  • Absolute: Overlying cellulitis or soft tissue infection — risk of seeding the joint
  • Relative: Prosthetic joint — aspiration still needed, but require orthopedics in sterile OR setting
  • Relative: Severe coagulopathy — bleeding from joint tap is rare; don't let this delay diagnosis of septic arthritis
  • Relative: Bacteremia — risk of seeding joint is theoretical; septic arthritis itself is an indication

General Principles

  • Use largest gauge needle that patient tolerates (18G preferred for large joints — viscous fluid requires larger bore)
  • Approach joint from extensor surface — fewer vessels and nerves on extensor side
  • Ultrasound guidance improves success rate for small or difficult joints
  • Do NOT inject steroids if septic arthritis suspected (diagnostic send first)

Knee (Most Common — Superolateral Approach)

  1. Patient supine, knee extended (fully); identify patella
  2. Enter at superolateral corner of patella, 1 cm lateral and 1 cm superior to superior-lateral border
  3. Sterile prep and drape; anesthetize skin with lidocaine (avoid intra-articular lidocaine if sending cultures)
  4. Insert 18G needle directing medially and slightly posteriorly, parallel to undersurface of patella
  5. Advance until pop is felt (entering joint space); aspirate — fluid should flow freely
  6. Aspirate as completely as possible; collect in appropriate tubes
  7. Remove needle; apply pressure and dressing

Ankle (Anteromedial Approach)

  1. Patient supine, ankle in slight plantarflexion; identify tibialis anterior tendon medially and medial malleolus
  2. Enter just medial to tibialis anterior tendon, above the joint line (palpate the joint line by plantarflexing/dorsiflexing)
  3. Direct needle posteriorly and slightly laterally into joint space
  4. Aspirate freely; US guidance recommended

Wrist (Dorsal Approach)

  1. Wrist slightly flexed over rolled towel; identify Lister's tubercle on distal radius
  2. Enter just distal and ulnar to Lister's tubercle, between extensor pollicis longus and extensor digitorum communis tendons
  3. Direct needle perpendicular to dorsal wrist surface; advance until joint entered

💡 No Fluid Aspirated?

Try: (1) redirect needle within joint space, (2) compress joint from opposite side to push fluid toward needle, (3) inject 2–3 mL saline and re-aspirate (dilutes fluid but still diagnostic), (4) ultrasound guidance for confirmation of position

Studies to Order

  • Always: Cell count with differential, Gram stain, culture (aerobic + anaerobic), crystal analysis (polarized microscopy)
  • If indicated: Glucose (compare to serum), AFB + fungal culture (chronic monoarthritis, immunocompromised)

Central Venous Catheter (CVC)

Central venous access for vasopressors, CVP monitoring, TPN, and medications that cannot be given peripherally. Seldinger technique.

Indications

  • Vasopressors / inotropes (all must go centrally)
  • Medications requiring central delivery: hypertonic saline (>3%), TPN, certain chemotherapy
  • No peripheral IV access (though IO or PICC preferred if subacute)
  • CVP monitoring / ScvO₂ sampling (sepsis resuscitation)
  • Hemodialysis (via non-tunneled dialysis catheter — larger bore, different placement)
  • Transvenous pacing wire delivery

Contraindications

  • Infection at planned insertion site
  • Known thrombosis at planned site
  • Subclavian: avoid in COPD/mechanically ventilated (higher pneumothorax risk), bilateral chest pathology, recent chest surgery
  • Femoral: avoid if possible — highest infection risk, avoid in ICU if alternatives exist

💡 Consider PICC First

If central access is needed but not urgently and vasopressors are not the indication, consider PICC (peripherally inserted central catheter) — lower infection risk than CVC, no pneumothorax risk, placed by IR or specialized nursing.

Setup

  • Patient supine, Trendelenburg 15–20° (distends IJ, reduces air embolism risk)
  • Head turned 30–45° away from insertion side (not >45° — distorts anatomy)
  • Ultrasound probe: identify IJ (lateral to carotid, compressible, no pulsation), carotid (medial, pulsatile, non-compressible)

Maximal Sterile Barriers

  • Surgical cap, mask, sterile gown, sterile gloves
  • Full-body sterile drape (not a small drape — full-length)
  • Skin: 2% chlorhexidine + 70% alcohol scrub; allow to dry completely (30–60 seconds)
  1. Set up sterile field; flush all ports of CVC with saline; place sterile ultrasound probe cover
  2. Identify IJ with real-time US; confirm vein vs artery (compress, visualize, use color Doppler)
  3. Anesthetize skin and subcutaneous tissue with 1% lidocaine at planned entry point
  4. Insert finder needle (21G) under real-time US guidance: visualize needle tip entering IJ lumen; aspirate dark venous blood freely
  5. Switch to 18G introducer needle through same entry point; confirm venous placement — should flow freely, blood should be dark (non-pulsatile)
  6. Verify venous placement BEFORE dilating: If uncertain, check with manometry (hang IV bag — blood should not flow back) or US confirmation of needle tip in vein
  7. Feed J-wire through introducer needle (should advance without resistance); maintain wire control at all times; remove introducer needle over wire
  8. Nick skin at wire entry with scalpel (#11 blade); pass dilator over wire with rotating motion (dilate subcutaneous tract — NOT the vein); remove dilator
  9. Feed CVC over wire; ensure wire exits distal port before fully advancing catheter; advance to appropriate depth (right IJ: 15–17 cm; left IJ: 17–19 cm)
  10. Remove wire completely; aspirate and flush all ports; secure catheter with suture or securement device
  11. Apply sterile occlusive dressing; CXR to confirm tip position (SVC-RA junction) and exclude pneumothorax

🚨 Wire Control — Never Let Go

Maintain control of the guidewire at ALL times. Lost wires require fluoroscopy for retrieval and have caused deaths. If wire is not visible exiting the catheter, stop — do not advance the catheter.

💡 Ideal Tip Position on CXR

SVC just above the RA — at or below the carina on CXR. Too deep (in RA or RV) → arrhythmia. Too proximal (above clavicle for subclavian) → won't measure accurate CVP.

Arterial Line

Continuous beat-to-beat blood pressure monitoring and arterial blood gas sampling access. Radial artery is first-line. Seldinger technique.

Indications

  • Hemodynamic instability requiring continuous real-time blood pressure monitoring (septic shock, cardiogenic shock, vasoactive infusions)
  • Frequent arterial blood gas sampling (>3–4/day)
  • Unreliable non-invasive blood pressure monitoring (arrhythmia, morbid obesity, peripheral vascular disease)
  • Anticipated major surgery (cardiac, vascular, thoracic)

Contraindications

  • Infection at insertion site
  • Raynaud disease or vasospastic disorder at planned site (relative)
  • Inadequate collateral circulation (failed Allen test — relative contraindication; significance debated)
  • Severe atherosclerosis at site (relative)
  • Hematoma or recent procedure at same site

💡 Allen Test (Modified)

Occlude both radial and ulnar arteries; patient opens/closes fist until blanched. Release ulnar artery only — palm should flush within 5–7 seconds if adequate collateral. Abnormal test: relative contraindication. However, studies show Allen test poorly predicts ischemic complications.

Site Preference

  • Radial (first choice): Accessible, compressible, dual blood supply via ulnar, low complication rate
  • Femoral (second choice): Larger vessel, easier in shock/poor perfusion; higher infection risk; limits mobility
  • Brachial (avoid if possible): No collateral — ischemic risk higher; use only if radial/femoral not feasible
  • Dorsalis pedis / posterior tibial: Alternative if radial unavailable; tracing often dampened

Procedure (Radial)

  1. Position wrist in 60° dorsiflexion over rolled towel; tape fingers to board to maintain position
  2. Palpate radial pulse at wrist; use ultrasound to identify radial artery (lateral to flexor carpi radialis tendon)
  3. Sterile prep and drape; anesthetize skin with 1% lidocaine (small wheal — don't inject into vessel)
  4. Insert 20G catheter-over-needle at 30–45° angle to skin, bevel up, directly over palpated pulse
  5. Advance until pulsatile blood flash appears in hub
  6. Seldinger technique: Flatten angle to 10°; advance guidewire through needle into vessel; remove needle over wire; advance catheter over wire into artery; remove wire
  7. OR direct cannulation: Advance catheter-over-needle through artery (until blood flash stops); withdraw needle, slowly retract catheter until blood flows; advance catheter forward
  8. Connect to pressurized arterial line tubing; confirm arterial waveform on monitor
  9. Secure with suture or securement device; sterile dressing

Intraosseous (IO) Access

Emergency vascular access via bone marrow cavity. Equivalent to central venous access for drug delivery. Use when IV access fails in time-critical situations.

Indications

  • Failed peripheral IV access in time-critical emergencies (cardiac arrest, septic shock, severe trauma)
  • 2 failed peripheral IV attempts in adult (<90 seconds) or 1 failed attempt in pediatric cardiac arrest
  • Immediate vascular access needed while CVC being set up

✓ IO = Central Access for Drug Delivery

All resuscitation medications — epinephrine, amiodarone, adenosine, vasopressors, fluids — can be given IO. Onset of action equivalent to central venous. Fluids must be given under pressure (IO does not flow by gravity).

Contraindications

  • Fracture at or proximal to planned insertion site
  • Previous IO attempt at same site within 48h (marrow cavity compromised)
  • Infection or burn at insertion site
  • Prosthetic joint at site
  • Osteogenesis imperfecta, osteoporosis (relative)
  • Bone metastases or osteomyelitis at site

Site Selection

  • Proximal tibia (first choice adults): Anteromedial surface, 2–3 cm distal to tibial tuberosity; flat, palpable surface; easy landmark
  • Distal tibia: Medial surface, 2–3 cm proximal to medial malleolus
  • Proximal humerus: Greater tubercle; highest flow rates; preferred if fluid resuscitation priority; harder landmark
  • Sternal (FAST-1 device): Manubrium; only for adults; specific device required

Procedure (EZ-IO Power Driver)

  1. Identify proximal tibia site: tibial tuberosity, go 2–3 cm distal, identify flat anteromedial surface
  2. Cleanse with chlorhexidine; sterile gloves
  3. In conscious patient: infiltrate skin and periosteum with 2% lidocaine (2 mL skin, 2 mL into marrow after confirmation)
  4. Select appropriate needle length (pink 15mm for <40 kg; blue 25mm standard adult; yellow 45mm obese/humerus)
  5. Hold EZ-IO driver like a drill perpendicular to bone surface; insert needle through skin; contact periosteum with firm pressure
  6. Activate drill; apply gentle downward pressure; advance needle until sudden decrease in resistance (entering marrow cavity); typically 2–4 cm advancement
  7. Remove stylet; attach syringe; aspirate marrow (confirms placement — may not always be possible); flush with 10 mL NS under pressure
  8. If conscious: inject 2 mL 2% lidocaine (preservative-free) slowly into marrow for analgesia; wait 60 seconds before bolusing medications
  9. Connect IO tubing; all fluids and medications must be given under pressure (pressure bag or push)
  10. Secure catheter; do not use IO >24h — convert to peripheral or central IV as soon as possible

💡 Confirming Placement

(1) Needle stands without support, (2) marrow aspirate, (3) no extravasation with 10 mL flush, (4) fluid flows freely under pressure, (5) patient response to medication. X-ray can confirm but don't delay resuscitation for imaging.

Airway Management

Sequential escalating approach to airway management. Know the algorithm before you need it. Preoxygenation is the single most important step before any intubation attempt.

Indications for Intubation (the 3 Ds)

  • Distressed / hypoxic breathing: SpO₂ <90% despite high-flow O₂, respiratory fatigue, worsening work of breathing
  • Depressed mental status: GCS ≤8 or inability to protect airway (aspiration risk)
  • Deteriorating / anticipated: Rapidly worsening condition — intubate before crisis (angioedema, epiglottitis, anaphylaxis, impending respiratory failure)

Relative Contraindications to RSI

  • Predicted difficult airway — have video laryngoscope ready, awake intubation, call anesthesia early
  • Hyperkalemia — succinylcholine causes K⁺ release (1 mEq/L); avoid if K⁺ >5.5, burns >24h, crush injury, denervation injury, prolonged immobility
  • Known allergy to RSI agents

🚨 Difficult Airway Prediction (LEMON)

Look externally (obesity, trauma, deformity)
Evaluate 3-3-2 (mouth opening 3 fingers, hyoid-chin 3 fingers, thyroid-floor 2 fingers)
Mallampati score ≥3
Obstruction (stridor, mass, foreign body)
Neck mobility (limited flexion/extension)

Step 1: Positioning & Basic Airway Maneuvers

  • Sniffing position: Head extended, neck flexed (pillow under occiput); aligns oral-pharyngeal-laryngeal axes
  • Jaw thrust: Place fingers behind angles of mandible, thrust jaw anteriorly — preferred over head-tilt-chin-lift if cervical spine injury suspected
  • Head-tilt chin-lift: Extend head, lift chin — opens upper airway in unconscious patient

Step 2: Oropharyngeal Airway (OPA)

  • For unconscious patients without gag reflex
  • Sizing: measure from corner of mouth to earlobe
  • Insert upside down (concave toward palate), rotate 180° as it passes posterior tongue; OR use tongue blade to depress tongue and insert directly
  • Do NOT use in conscious patients — triggers vomiting

Step 3: Bag-Valve-Mask (BVM) Ventilation

  1. Connect BVM to 100% O₂ at 15 L/min; attach reservoir bag
  2. Place OPA (if no gag); position patient (sniffing position); head tilt-chin lift or jaw thrust
  3. EC clamp: thumb + index finger form "C" around mask; remaining fingers form "E" under mandible (lift jaw up into mask)
  4. Two-provider technique preferred: one holds mask with both hands (E-C clamp both sides), second squeezes bag — superior seal
  5. Tidal volume: squeeze bag until chest rises (approximately 500–600 mL for average adult) — avoid over-ventilation
  6. Rate: 10–12 breaths/min (1 breath every 5–6 seconds); during CPR: 10 breaths/min
  7. Monitor: chest rise, SpO₂, ETCO₂ if available, gastric distension

Step 4: RSI (Rapid Sequence Intubation)

  1. Preparation: Equipment check (blade, ETT, stylet, suction, BVM, ETCO₂, confirm IV access)
  2. Preoxygenation: NRB mask at 15 L/min × 3–5 minutes OR 8 vital capacity breaths; target SpO₂ >95% before paralysis; consider high-flow NC during laryngoscopy (apneic oxygenation)
  3. Positioning: Sniffing position; head-of-bed elevation 20–30° (ramp obese patients with blankets)
  4. Pretreatment (optional, 3 min before induction): Lidocaine 1.5 mg/kg IV (reactive airway disease); fentanyl 3 mcg/kg IV (blunt sympathetic response in TBI/elevated ICP)
  5. Induction agent: Ketamine 1.5 mg/kg IV (preferred in hypotension, bronchospasm, status asthmaticus) OR Etomidate 0.3 mg/kg IV (stable BP, but adrenal suppression noted with sepsis) OR Propofol 1.5–2 mg/kg IV (avoid if SBP <100)
  6. Paralytic: Succinylcholine 1.5 mg/kg IV (unless contraindicated — see above; onset 45–60 sec) OR Rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg IV (duration 60–90 min; reversal with sugammadex available)
  7. Intubation at 45–60 seconds: Laryngoscope blade in right side of mouth, sweep tongue left; direct or video laryngoscopy — identify epiglottis, vallecular, glottis, vocal cords
  8. Pass ETT: Advance tube through cords under visualization; inflate cuff; confirm with waveform ETCO₂ (gold standard), bilateral breath sounds, CXR (tip 2–4 cm above carina)
  9. Post-intubation: Long-acting sedation + analgesia (fentanyl + midazolam or propofol); post-intubation CXR; secure tube; document ETT depth at teeth

Failed Airway — Rescue Sequence

  • Attempt 1 fails → optimize (position, suction, external laryngeal manipulation, BURP)
  • Attempt 2 fails → video laryngoscopy if not already using; call for help NOW
  • Cannot intubate, CAN oxygenate → supraglottic airway (LMA, King LT) as bridge; call anesthesia/ENT
  • Cannot intubate, CANNOT oxygenate → cricothyrotomy (surgical airway) immediately — this is a life-saving procedure

Nasogastric (NG) Tube

Gastric decompression, enteral feeding, medication delivery, and GI bleed management. Confirm placement before any use.

Indications

  • Decompression: Bowel obstruction, gastroparesis, post-operative ileus, paralytic ileus
  • Enteral nutrition: Unable to swallow or inadequate oral intake; short-term feeding (prolonged → consider PEG/NJ)
  • Medication delivery: Altered mental status, dysphagia, oral medications in intubated patient
  • Gastric lavage: Toxin ingestion (limited evidence; selected cases only), GI bleed visualization/clot clearance
  • Charcoal administration (acute poisoning within 1–2 hours)

Absolute Contraindications

  • Facial trauma / basilar skull fracture — risk of intracranial placement (use oral route)
  • Known or suspected esophageal perforation
  • Recent esophageal or gastric surgery with anastomosis

Relative Contraindications

  • Esophageal varices (large) — use cautiously, smaller tube; benefit may outweigh risk in active GI bleed
  • Coagulopathy (epistaxis risk)
  • Uncooperative patient
  • Nasal obstruction, deviated septum (use other nostril or oral route)

Tube Selection

  • Decompression / large bore drainage: Salem Sump (18F); double lumen (drainage + vent port); better suctioning
  • Enteral feeding: Small bore (8–12F Dobhoff/Corpak); more comfortable, confirmation needed (X-ray)
  • Medication / suction (short-term): Standard 16–18F single-lumen NG

Estimating Insertion Length

  • Measure: nose → earlobe → xiphoid process (NEX method) — typically 45–55 cm in adult
  • Mark the tube at this length
  1. Sit patient upright at 45–90° if possible; examine nostrils, choose most patent side
  2. Lubricate distal 15 cm of tube with water-soluble lubricant (or topical 2% lidocaine jelly for comfort)
  3. Apply topical anesthetic spray to posterior pharynx (lidocaine or benzocaine — if no allergy)
  4. Insert tube along floor of nasal passage (NOT upward — floor is horizontal), advancing posteriorly toward nasopharynx
  5. When tube reaches posterior pharynx (patient may feel gagging), ask patient to flex neck slightly and swallow; advance with each swallow
  6. Continue advancing to marked length; if resistance encountered, try other nostril or oral route
  7. Confirm placement BEFORE ANY USE (see below)
  8. Secure tube to nose with tape; connect to drainage bag or suction

🚨 Stop Immediately If:

  • Coughing, gagging, cyanosis, SpO₂ dropping — may indicate tracheal placement
  • Resistance that does not resolve — esophageal or pharyngeal resistance
  • Patient unable to speak during advancement (tube in trachea)

Confirming Placement

  • Gold standard: X-ray (CXR or AXR) — visualize tube coursing below diaphragm, tip in gastric body. Required for small-bore feeding tubes before first use.
  • Auscultation (insufflation test): Inject 20–30 mL air while auscultating over epigastrium — whooshing sound heard. NOT reliable alone — cannot exclude esophageal or bronchial placement
  • Aspiration of gastric contents: pH <5 strongly suggests gastric position; pH >6 — reconsider placement
  • Capnometry: Absence of CO₂ on colorimetric cap suggests non-respiratory placement

Clinical rule: Always confirm small-bore feeding tube placement with X-ray before first use. For large-bore Salem sumps, auscultation + aspiration of gastric contents is generally accepted in stable patients — but X-ray is safest.

Urinary Catheter (Foley)

Bladder drainage via urethral catheterization. Strict aseptic technique and daily necessity review are the cornerstones of CAUTI prevention.

Acceptable Indications (CDC / HICPAC)

  • Urinary retention or bladder outlet obstruction
  • Critical illness requiring strict hourly urine output monitoring (septic shock, acute tubular necrosis, cardiogenic shock)
  • Perioperative use for selected surgeries (not routine for all surgeries)
  • Stage III–IV sacral/perineal wound management to protect from urine contamination
  • Patient comfort in end-of-life care (immobilized, distressing urinary incontinence)
  • Hematuria with clot retention (three-way catheter for irrigation)

⚠️ NOT Acceptable Indications

  • Urinary incontinence alone (use pads or condom catheter)
  • Patient or nursing staff convenience
  • Routine post-op without specific indication
  • Urine culture collection (use clean-catch or in-and-out catheter)

Contraindications

  • Urethral trauma / disruption: Blood at meatus, perineal hematoma, high-riding or non-palpable prostate on rectal exam → urology emergently before any catheter attempt
  • Known urethral stricture (relative — use flexible cystoscopy or council-tip catheter)
  • Recent urethral or prostate surgery (relative — consult urology)

Catheter Selection

  • Standard Foley: 14–18F (adult); use smallest that drains adequately
  • Three-way catheter: 20–22F; for continuous bladder irrigation (hematuria, clot retention)
  • Coudé tip: Curved tip for BPH or urethral strictures — insert with tip pointing upward

Sterile Technique — Female

  1. Patient supine, knees bent, hips externally rotated (frog-leg position)
  2. Open sterile catheter kit; don sterile gloves; set up sterile field
  3. Drape patient with fenestrated sterile drape
  4. With non-dominant hand, separate labia minora to expose urethral meatus — this hand is now contaminated (do not use for sterile field)
  5. With dominant hand, use antiseptic swabs (povidone-iodine or chlorhexidine) to cleanse: center → left labium → right labium (one swipe each, inner to outer, discard each swab)
  6. Apply lubricant to catheter tip (2–5 cm); insert catheter gently into urethra (3–5 cm in females); advance until urine returns
  7. Advance catheter an additional 2–3 cm after urine return before inflating balloon
  8. Inflate balloon with 10 mL sterile water (as labeled on catheter); withdraw until resistance felt
  9. Connect to closed drainage system; secure catheter to inner thigh

Sterile Technique — Male

  1. Patient supine; hold penis at 90° angle (perpendicular to bed) to straighten urethra
  2. Retract foreskin fully if uncircumcised; replace after procedure
  3. With non-dominant hand, hold penis (now contaminated hand); cleanse meatus with antiseptic in circular motion (inside-out), 3 swipes, new swab each time
  4. Instill 10 mL 2% lidocaine jelly into urethra via syringe; hold at meatus 3–5 minutes (improves success rate, reduces discomfort)
  5. Advance lubricated catheter gently (15–22 cm in males) until urine returns; if resistance at prostate level: increase traction on penis (further straightens urethra), or try Coudé catheter
  6. Advance 2–3 cm further after urine; inflate balloon with 10 mL sterile water
  7. Reduce foreskin to prevent paraphimosis; connect drainage bag; secure

💡 No Urine Return After Insertion?

(1) Advance further — may not be in bladder yet. (2) Flush with 30–60 mL sterile saline (obese patients — bladder may be displaced). (3) Ensure drainage bag below bladder level. (4) Ultrasound to confirm bladder has urine. (5) Consult urology if still no return.

CAUTI Prevention Bundle

  • Limit catheter insertion to accepted indications only
  • Maintain closed drainage system — never disconnect unless necessary
  • Keep drainage bag below bladder level at all times — never above patient
  • Daily necessity review: "Does this patient still need the catheter?" Remove as soon as indication resolves
  • Secure catheter to thigh/abdomen — prevents urethral trauma from traction
  • Perineal hygiene daily with soap and water — no antiseptic creams (no proven benefit, may worsen)
  • Automatic stop orders at 48–72h (institution-dependent) or nurse-driven removal protocols
  • Do not irrigate unless obstruction by clots

Synchronized DC Cardioversion

Delivery of a synchronized shock timed to the R-wave to terminate tachyarrhythmias. Synchronization prevents R-on-T phenomenon and ventricular fibrillation.

Indications — Emergent (Unstable)

  • Any tachyarrhythmia with hemodynamic instability: hypotension (SBP <90), altered mental status, chest pain, pulmonary edema, or signs of shock
  • Applies to: AFib, Aflutter, SVT, VT with pulse
  • Do NOT delay for anticoagulation in unstable patient

Indications — Elective

  • AFib <48h duration (or on therapeutic anticoagulation ≥3 weeks, or TEE negative for LA thrombus)
  • Aflutter (very responsive — often 50J converts)
  • Symptomatic SVT refractory to vagal maneuvers and adenosine
  • Hemodynamically stable VT refractory to antiarrhythmics

Contraindications

  • AFib with duration >48h without anticoagulation for ≥3 weeks and no TEE to rule out LA thrombus (elective only — emergency overrides)
  • Digitalis toxicity — cardioversion may cause refractory VF; correct toxicity first
  • Sinus tachycardia (underlying cause must be treated)
  • Multifocal atrial tachycardia (MAP) — cannot cardiovert; treat underlying cause
  • Implanted pacemaker/ICD (relative — place pads away from device; may damage ICD)

🚨 Do Not Cardiovert VF or Pulseless VT

These require defibrillation (unsynchronized), not cardioversion. For any pulseless arrhythmia → follow ACLS algorithm: CPR + defibrillation.

Pre-Procedure (Elective)

  • Anticoagulation: AFib >48h (or unknown duration) requires ≥3 weeks therapeutic anticoagulation OR TEE to rule out LA thrombus before elective cardioversion
  • Post-cardioversion anticoagulation: Continue for minimum 4 weeks after (atrial stunning); longer if CHA₂DS₂-VASc ≥2
  • NPO for conscious sedation (ideally >4h, but don't delay if urgent)
  • Electrolytes: correct hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia (increases success rate)
  • IV access; 12-lead ECG to confirm rhythm

Equipment Setup

  • Defibrillator with SYNC mode available and confirmed active (should see sync marker on R-wave on rhythm strip)
  • Oxygen source, BVM, suction, emergency airway available
  • Sedation/analgesia drawn up (procedural sedation team or physician anesthesia)
  • Defibrillator pads: anterior-posterior (AP) preferred (higher success); or anterior-lateral if AP not feasible
  1. Apply monitoring leads; confirm arrhythmia on monitor; apply defibrillator pads (or paddles with gel)
  2. Activate SYNC mode on defibrillator; confirm R-wave markers visible on display
  3. Administer procedural sedation: Midazolam 1–2 mg IV + fentanyl 1–2 mcg/kg IV; or propofol 0.5–1 mg/kg IV (have airway support ready)
  4. Select energy: (see table below)
  5. Confirm all personnel clear of patient, bed, equipment ("I'm clear, you're clear, everyone clear")

  7. Immediately assess rhythm and pulse; if unsuccessful, increase energy and repeat
  8. After conversion, confirm sinus rhythm on 12-lead ECG; monitor closely for 1–2h
  9. Reactivate SYNC mode before each subsequent shock — most defibrillators revert to ASYNC after each shock

Transcutaneous Pacing (TCP)

Non-invasive emergency cardiac pacing via external electrical stimulation through the chest wall. Bridge to transvenous pacing or resolution of reversible cause. Painful in conscious patients — analgesia and sedation are essential.

Indications

  • Symptomatic bradycardia with hemodynamic compromise (hypotension, AMS, chest pain, pulmonary edema) unresponsive to atropine
  • Complete heart block (3rd degree AV block) — acute MI (especially inferior STEMI), Lyme carditis, drug toxicity
  • High-degree AV block (Mobitz II) — risk of progression to complete block
  • Bradycardia with escape rhythm refractory to medical therapy
  • Sinus arrest / sick sinus syndrome with hemodynamic instability
  • Bridge while awaiting transvenous pacemaker placement
  • Overdrive pacing for torsades de pointes (rate 100–120 bpm)

Contraindications

  • Hypothermia with bradycardia — TCP often ineffective; rewarm first
  • Severe hypothermia — prone to VF with stimulation; rewarm to >30°C before pacing
  • Asystole (without P-waves) — TCP rarely effective; continue ACLS
  • Chest wall trauma at pad sites (relative)

💡 Atropine First

For symptomatic bradycardia: atropine 0.5 mg IV q3–5 min (max 3 mg) is first-line. TCP if atropine fails or is ineffective (complete AV block — atropine works on SA node, not infranodal block). Start TCP while atropine is being given in severely unstable patients — don't wait.

Setup

  • Attach defibrillator/pacer monitor leads (requires 4-lead monitoring during TCP)
  • Apply pacing pads (same pads as defibrillation):
    • Anterior-posterior (preferred): Anterior pad left parasternal 4th ICS; posterior pad left scapula
    • Alternative: Standard defibrillation positions (right parasternal 2nd ICS; left mid-axillary 4th–5th ICS)
  • Ensure good pad contact; shave chest hair if needed for contact

Analgesia / Sedation (Critical — Awake Patient)

  • TCP is extremely painful in conscious patients (skeletal muscle contraction with each impulse)
  • Fentanyl 1–2 mcg/kg IV + midazolam 1–2 mg IV; or morphine 2–4 mg IV
  • Do not delay pacing for sedation if patient is critically unstable — pace first, sedate immediately after
  1. Connect pacing pads to defibrillator in PACING mode; connect monitoring leads
  2. Set rate: 60–80 bpm (overdrive pacing for torsades: 100–120 bpm)
  3. Start output (mA) low (50 mA in conscious patients to minimize pain initially) and increase by 10 mA increments until electrical capture
  4. In unconscious/pulseless patients: start at maximum output (200 mA) and titrate down
  5. Electrical capture: Pacing spike followed by wide QRS complex on monitor — every spike captures
  6. Confirm mechanical capture: Palpate femoral or brachial pulse (do NOT use carotid — cannot distinguish pacing artifact from true pulse). SpO₂ waveform should correlate. BP should improve.
  7. Add 10% above threshold output for safety margin (e.g., captures at 80 mA → set at 90 mA)
  8. Administer analgesia/sedation; monitor continuously
  9. Arrange urgent transvenous pacing (TCP is a bridge only) and cardiology consultation

⚠️ Electrical vs Mechanical Capture

Electrical capture (pacing spikes + QRS on monitor) does NOT guarantee mechanical capture (cardiac output). Always confirm with pulse palpation or arterial line waveform. Pseudo-PEA can occur — large QRS complexes from pacing artifact with no actual cardiac contraction.

Point-of-Care Ultrasound (POCUS)

Bedside diagnostic ultrasound performed and interpreted by the treating physician in real time. ACGME IM Milestone v2.0 requirement. Not a comprehensive study — targeted to answer specific clinical questions.

Clinical Applications for Hospitalists

  • Volume status: IVC diameter and collapsibility
  • Cardiac: Gross LV/RV function, pericardial effusion, tamponade physiology
  • Pulmonary: Pleural effusion, pneumothorax, interstitial edema (B-lines), lung consolidation
  • Abdominal: Ascites confirmation and pocket assessment before paracentesis
  • DVT assessment: Compression ultrasound of femoral and popliteal veins
  • Procedure guidance: Paracentesis, thoracentesis, central line, arthrocentesis, LP (pediatric)
  • Hypotension / shock: RUSH protocol (see below)

Marker convention: Probe indicator (notch or light) corresponds to left side of screen in standard orientation. Cardiac: marker toward right shoulder. Abdominal/vascular: marker toward patient's head or right side.

Technique

  1. Patient supine; phased array or curvilinear probe at subxiphoid / epigastric position
  2. Angle probe toward right atrium; identify IVC as anechoic tubular structure to right of aorta, draining into RA
  3. Confirm IVC (not aorta): IVC compressible, no pulsation, flows into RA; aorta pulsatile, non-compressible, branches into iliac vessels
  4. Measure maximum IVC diameter in M-mode or 2D, 2 cm from RA-IVC junction, during quiet breathing
  5. Assess collapsibility with sniff or deep inspiration

Technique (2-Point Compression)

  1. Linear probe (7–15 MHz); patient supine with leg externally rotated; slight knee bend
  2. Point 1 — Common Femoral Vein (CFV): Identify femoral vessels at groin (inguinal ligament); vein medial to artery; compress with probe pressure — normal vein collapses completely
  3. Point 2 — Popliteal Vein: Patient prone or leg flexed; identify popliteal fossa; vein superficial to artery; compress
  4. 3-point: also compress femoral vein at mid-thigh

Interpretation

  • Normal: Vein fully compressible — walls meet with gentle probe pressure
  • DVT: Vein non-compressible or incompletely compressible; may visualize echogenic thrombus within lumen
  • Sensitivity 94–96% for proximal DVT; 60–70% for calf DVT (calf not routinely assessed with 2-point)

⚠️ Limitations

2-point compression misses isolated iliac and calf DVTs. If high pretest probability and negative 2-point: formal venous duplex ultrasound or repeat in 5–7 days. POCUS DVT exam does not replace formal vascular lab study in low-probability or complex cases.

Pigtail Catheter & PleurX (Tunneled Pleural Catheter)

Small-bore pleural drainage for empyema, large effusions, and recurrent malignant effusions. Pigtail (8–14F) has largely replaced large-bore chest tubes for most medical indications.

Pigtail Catheter (8–14F) — Indications

  • Empyema or complicated parapneumonic effusion (pH <7.2, glucose <60, positive culture)
  • Large symptomatic effusion requiring ongoing drainage
  • Malignant effusion (bridge to PleurX or pleurodesis)
  • Pneumothorax requiring drainage (>20% or symptomatic) — 8–14F sufficient for most spontaneous PTX

PleurX Tunneled Pleural Catheter — Indications

  • Recurrent malignant pleural effusion — preferred over repeat thoracentesis or pleurodesis in patients with trapped lung or limited prognosis
  • Refractory hepatic hydrothorax (alternative to TIPS)
  • Allows outpatient self-drainage every 1–3 days; median time to spontaneous pleurodesis ~50 days

Contraindications

  • Coagulopathy uncorrected (relative — same thresholds as thoracentesis)
  • Overlying skin infection at planned site
  • Fibrothorax / trapped lung — drainage will not expand lung; discuss goals with patient
  • PleurX: active pleural infection (use pigtail first)
  1. Ultrasound to identify largest pocket, measure depth, mark site; sterile prep, drape, gloves
  2. Anesthetize skin and pleura with lidocaine; confirm fluid with finder needle before proceeding
  3. Insert introducer needle over top of rib; advance aspirating until fluid returns
  4. Feed J-wire through needle into pleural space; remove needle, maintain wire control
  5. Nick skin with scalpel at wire entry; pass dilator over wire to create tract; remove dilator
  6. Thread pigtail catheter over wire; advance until all side holes are inside pleural space; remove wire — pigtail curls within space
  7. Connect to water-seal drainage system (Pleur-evac); confirm drainage flowing; secure with suture
  8. CXR to confirm position and assess lung re-expansion
  9. For empyema: consider intrapleural tPA + DNase (Alteplase 10 mg + Dornase alfa 5 mg in 30 mL NS twice daily × 3 days — MIST2 trial)

💡 Empyema — Intrapleural Fibrinolytics

MIST2 trial (NEJM 2011): tPA + DNase twice daily × 3 days significantly reduced need for surgery and hospital stay vs placebo. Standard of care for loculated/complex empyema not draining adequately. Contraindicated if bronchopleural fistula (large air leak).

Chest Tube Troubleshooting

Management of existing chest tubes — air leaks, poor drainage, tube repositioning, and safe removal. Hospitalists manage these daily even without placing them.

Ideal Tube Position on CXR

  • Pneumothorax: Apex of hemithorax; tip pointing superiorly
  • Effusion / empyema: Base of hemithorax; tip in most dependent portion
  • All side holes must be inside the pleural space — side hole outside = subcutaneous emphysema + ineffective drainage
  • Tube should not cross midline or be in a fissure (causes poor drainage)

Localizing the Leak

  1. Confirm bubbling in water seal chamber during expiration (or continuously)
  2. Check all connections from patient to collection chamber — temporarily occlude each with clamp, moving proximally; if bubbling stops → leak is proximal to that point
  3. Inspect insertion site — loose suture or subcutaneous air may allow air ingress
  4. If all external connections intact → air leak is from lung parenchyma (bronchopleural fistula)

🚨 Never Clamp a Tube with Active Air Leak

Clamping a tube with Grade 3–4 air leak can cause tension pneumothorax. Only clamp briefly for leak localization, then unclamp immediately.

  1. Check tubing: kinked? Dependent loops? Clamped? Connections tight?
  2. Check water-seal system: upright? Water level correct?
  3. Milk tubing gently to dislodge clots — do NOT strip (generates dangerous negative pressure)
  4. Flush with 20–30 mL sterile NS (confirm with team for post-op tubes)
  5. CXR: tube malpositioned? In fissure? Side holes inside chest? Residual fluid present?
  6. CT chest if CXR inconclusive — best for assessing loculation and tube position
  7. Intrapleural fibrinolytics for empyema/loculated effusion (tPA + DNase)

  • Criteria: Pneumothorax: no air leak 24h on water seal + lung expanded. Effusion: <100–150 mL/24h. Empyema: clinical improvement + <50 mL/day.

Temporary Transvenous Pacemaker

Definitive temporary pacing via pacing wire to right ventricle. Hospitalists must recognize capture, sensing failure, and complications — even if cardiology places the wire.

Indications

  • Symptomatic bradycardia / complete AV block unresponsive to medical therapy and TCP
  • Acute MI with complete heart block, new bilateral BBB, new Mobitz II with BBB
  • Overdrive pacing for refractory torsades de pointes or pause-dependent VT
  • Post-cardiac surgery bridge
  • Prophylactic pre-procedure in patients with LBBB undergoing right heart catheterization

Contraindications

  • Tricuspid prosthetic valve (wire cannot cross safely)
  • Coagulopathy (relative — use femoral if coagulopathic)

Finding Capture Threshold

  1. Set rate above intrinsic rate; confirm 100% pacing
  2. Start output high (5 mA); confirm capture (spike + QRS + pulse)
  3. Gradually reduce output until capture is lost
  4. Threshold = lowest mA with consistent capture
  5. Set final output at 3× threshold (e.g., threshold 1.5 mA → set 4.5 mA)

Failure to Capture (spike without QRS)

  • Increase output (mA) — may be below threshold
  • Wire displacement (most common) — CXR; reposition under fluoroscopy
  • RV perforation — echo to check for effusion/tamponade
  • Hyperkalemia — raises capture threshold; correct electrolytes

Failure to Sense — Undersensing (fires despite intrinsic beat)

  • Decrease sensitivity (lower mV) — dangerous: R-on-T risk
  • Wire displacement reducing electrogram amplitude

Oversensing (inappropriately inhibited)

  • Increase sensitivity value (mV); check for electromagnetic interference
  • If life-threatening bradycardia: switch to asynchronous mode (VOO) temporarily

Paced ECG Pattern

  • RV apex pacing: spike + wide LBBB-morphology QRS (negative V1, positive V6)
  • RBBB morphology = wire in coronary sinus — needs repositioning

Incision & Drainage (I&D)

Definitive treatment for cutaneous abscess. Antibiotics alone are insufficient — drainage is required. NEJM 2017: TMP-SMX after I&D reduces treatment failure and recurrence.

Indications

  • Any fluctuant cutaneous abscess — drainage is mandatory
  • Furunculosis (folliculitis with central fluctuance)
  • Post-surgical wound abscess

🚨 Danger Triangle of Face & High-Risk Sites

Nasal bridge → corners of mouth — venous drainage connects to cavernous sinus → cavernous sinus thrombosis risk. Also: hand infections (tendon sheath), perirectal, deep neck. Refer these to surgery/ENT — do NOT drain at bedside.

Contraindications

  • Non-fluctuant cellulitis — antibiotics + warm compresses; US to confirm no occult abscess
  • Periorbital, nasal bridge, upper lip (danger triangle) — refer
  • Suspected vascular aneurysm mimicking abscess — US/CT before any incision
  1. Confirm fluctuance — palpate or US (anechoic collection with posterior acoustic enhancement)
  2. Anesthesia: field block with 1% lidocaine + epinephrine around abscess perimeter (not into cavity); consider procedural sedation for large painful abscesses
  3. Incision: single linear incision with #11 blade over point of maximum fluctuance; length should span full abscess cavity
  4. Express pus; culture if: immunocompromised, severe infection, recurrent, or MRSA risk
  5. Break loculations: curved hemostat in cavity, open/spread in multiple directions; run finger through to ensure fully deroofed
  6. Copious irrigation with NS via syringe + angiocath until return is clear
  7. Loose packing with iodoform/plain gauze ribbon; leave visible tail 2–3 cm outside skin
  8. Cover with dry gauze; follow up in 24–48h for packing removal

💡 Loop Drainage (Alternative)

Two small stab incisions on opposite sides; pass vessel loop through; tie loosely. Patient showers; loop wicks drainage. Remove at 7–10 days. Avoids packing removal visits.

Antibiotics After I&D

  • NEJM 2017 (Talan): TMP-SMX 160/800 mg BID × 7d after I&D → reduced treatment failure 7.4% vs 17.6%
  • Antibiotics indicated if: Abscess >2 cm, multiple abscesses, surrounding cellulitis >2 cm, immunocompromised, systemic signs, face/hands/genitalia

  • Exudate: Amount and character (serous, serosanguinous, purulent)
  • Periwound: Erythema (cellulitis = firm/indurated vs maceration = soft/white/moist), edema, warmth
  • Signs of infection: Increasing pain, erythema, warmth, purulent drainage, fever, failure to progress 2–4 weeks

Skin Punch Biopsy

Full-thickness skin biopsy for histopathologic diagnosis of rashes, vasculitis, and bullous disease. Commonly performed by hospitalists before or during dermatology consultation.

Indications

  • Undiagnosed rash requiring histopathology (vasculitis, drug reaction, neutrophilic dermatoses, AGEP, SJS/TEN)
  • Bullous disease (pemphigus, bullous pemphigoid, DH)
  • Infectious etiology requiring tissue culture (fungal, atypical mycobacteria)
  • Febrile illness with rash of unknown etiology (inpatient workup before derm consult)

Contraindications

  • Actively infected site; bleeding diathesis (relative); keloid-prone sites (chest, shoulders)

Site Selection for Maximum Yield

  • Vasculitis / purpura: Early active lesion (<24–48h) — older lesions show only non-specific changes
  • Bullous disease: Edge of intact blister — include blister edge + adjacent normal skin
  • DIF specimen: Perilesional normal skin (2 cm from lesion) — NOT the lesion itself; send in Michel's medium
  • Avoid: palms, soles, face (cosmesis)

💡 DIF — Michel's Medium, NOT Formalin

Direct immunofluorescence requires fresh tissue in Michel's medium. Formalin destroys antibodies. Always send TWO separate specimens if ordering H&E + DIF — different fixatives.

Equipment

  • 4 mm punch (standard); 3 mm for face; 6 mm for bullous disease
  • Formalin (H&E) and Michel's medium (DIF) containers as needed
  • 1% lidocaine + epinephrine; curved iris scissors; 4-0 nylon suture
  1. Identify and mark target lesion; cleanse with alcohol
  2. Infiltrate 1% lidocaine + epi; wait 3–5 min for vasoconstriction
  3. Stretch skin perpendicular to relaxed skin tension lines (RSTL) — creates elliptical defect that closes more easily
  4. Place punch perpendicular to skin; firm downward pressure with rotating twisting motion until feel the "give" entering subcutaneous fat (~3–4 mm depth)
  5. Grasp specimen at dermal side with forceps (do not crush epidermis); snip base with curved scissors at subcutaneous fat level
  6. Place immediately in appropriate fixative; send for histology ± culture ± DIF
  7. Hemostasis: direct pressure ± aluminum chloride/Monsel's solution; or 4-0 nylon simple suture (remove 7–14 days per site)

Intra-Articular Knee Injection

Corticosteroid injection for OA flares, crystal arthropathy, and inflammatory arthritis. Drain first if effusion present, then inject through the same needle.

Indications

  • OA flare with effusion — short-term pain relief and function improvement
  • Acute gout or pseudogout when systemic therapy contraindicated
  • Inflammatory arthritis (RA, psoriatic) — bridge to systemic therapy

🚨 Rule Out Septic Arthritis First

NEVER inject corticosteroids without aspirating fluid for analysis if any clinical concern for infection. Steroid injection into a septic joint accelerates cartilage destruction.

Contraindications

  • Septic arthritis — absolute
  • Overlying cellulitis — absolute
  • Prosthetic joint — refer to orthopedics; OR environment required
  • Uncontrolled diabetes — relative; transient hyperglycemia for days; counsel and monitor
  • Limit: 3–4 injections per joint per year (McAlindon NEJM 2017: accelerated cartilage loss with quarterly triamcinolone)

Often mixed with 1–4 mL of 1% lidocaine for immediate pain relief and to confirm intra-articular placement.

  1. Position patient supine, knee fully extended; use superolateral approach (same as arthrocentesis)
  2. Sterile prep and drape; anesthetize skin
  3. If draining effusion first: aspirate completely with 18G needle; SWITCH TO NEW SYRINGE (leaving needle in place); attach steroid syringe; inject without repositioning needle
  4. If injection only: 21–22G needle at superolateral patella border; advance into joint space
  5. Inject smoothly — no resistance if intra-articular; subcutaneous resistance = misplaced, reposition
  6. Remove needle; flex/extend knee 5–10× to distribute steroid; bandage
  7. Counsel: avoid heavy activity 24–48h; may have flare 6–24h (resolves 72h); full effect 3–7 days

💡 Post-Injection Flare vs Infection

Flare: onset 6–24h, resolves by 72h, no fever. Septic joint: gradual onset, worsening, fever, systemic toxicity. Worsening beyond 72h or systemic signs → return for aspiration.

Bone Marrow Biopsy & Aspiration

Tissue sampling for hematologic diagnosis. Aspiration (liquid) and core biopsy (solid cylinder) are almost always performed together. Posterior iliac crest is the preferred site.

Indications

  • Unexplained cytopenias — pancytopenia, isolated anemia, thrombocytopenia, leukopenia
  • Suspected hematologic malignancy — leukemia, lymphoma staging, myeloma (plasma cell %), MDS
  • Staging of solid tumors (small cell lung, breast, neuroblastoma)
  • Unexplained leukocytosis with blasts on peripheral smear
  • Fever of unknown origin — granulomatous disease, disseminated infection, HLH
  • Iron stores assessment (definitive iron deficiency diagnosis)

Contraindications

  • Overlying infection at site
  • Severe thrombocytopenia (<20,000) or coagulopathy — relative; may still be needed; correct first with hematology guidance
  • Prior pelvic radiation — fibrous marrow, poor yield; use sternum (aspiration only) or anterior iliac crest

Positioning & Landmarks

  • Patient prone or lateral decubitus
  • PSIS (posterior superior iliac spine) = dimple above buttocks; entry point 2–3 cm lateral and inferior to PSIS on flat iliac surface

Anesthesia

  • 1% lidocaine + epi: skin wheal + advance to periosteum; inject 3–5 mL at periosteum; wait 3–5 min
  • Procedural sedation for anxious patients: midazolam 1–2 mg + fentanyl 1–2 mcg/kg IV
  1. Sterile prep and drape; confirm position and landmarks
  2. Aspiration first: Jamshidi needle perpendicular to bone with firm rotational pressure; feel cortex penetration; remove stylet; attach syringe
  3. Aspirate RAPIDLY (fast aspiration improves yield) — 0.3–0.5 mL; patient feels sharp pressure 1–2 seconds (warn them)
  4. Distribute aspirate: 2 drops on each of 4–6 slides (air dry immediately); additional aliquots per lab requirements
  5. Core biopsy: Advance Jamshidi 2–3 cm deeper with rotation; rotate 360° to sever core; withdraw with rotating backward motion to retain core
  6. Expel core through DISTAL end (not through cutting tip); touch prep (imprint) slides before placing in formalin
  7. Apply pressure 5–10 min; dressing; ambulate after 30–60 min

⚠️ Dry Tap

No marrow aspirated. Causes: myelofibrosis, densely packed marrow (leukemia), poor technique. Management: (1) reposition needle, (2) move to new site, (3) rely on core + touch preps. Dry tap itself is diagnostic for myelofibrosis.
