Principles of De-escalation

Goal: Narrow spectrum based on culture/susceptibility data to reduce collateral damage (C. diff, resistance, toxicity) while maintaining efficacy.
‚úÖ When to de-escalate:
  • Culture and susceptibility data available (typically 48-72h)
  • Clinical improvement observed
  • Source of infection identified

‚ö†Ô∏è Discontinue empiric MRSA coverage if:
  • MRSA nasal swab negative (NPV >95% for MRSA pneumonia)
  • Cultures negative for MRSA at 48-72h
  • MSSA isolated (switch to beta-lactam)

Pseudomonas De-escalation

‚úÖ Discontinue empiric Pseudomonas coverage if:
  • Cultures grow susceptible non-Pseudomonas organism
  • Respiratory cultures negative at 48-72h in low-risk patient
  • Clinical improvement with source identified as non-pulmonary

Criteria for IV-to-PO Switch

‚úÖ Patient is ready for PO antibiotics when:
  • Clinical improvement (afebrile, hemodynamically stable)
  • Functioning GI tract (tolerating PO intake)
  • No indication requiring IV (endocarditis, meningitis, severe sepsis)

üî¨ Culture & Diagnostic Stewardship

‚ñº

When to Obtain Cultures

‚úÖ Always obtain cultures before antibiotics for:
  • Sepsis / septic shock
  • Pneumonia (hospitalized patients)
  • Endocarditis (3 sets from separate sites)
  • Meningitis
  • Osteomyelitis / septic arthritis
  • Complicated UTI / pyelonephritis

When NOT to Culture

‚ö†Ô∏è Avoid cultures that rarely change management:
  • Uncomplicated cystitis in young women
  • Asymptomatic bacteriuria (except pregnancy, pre-urologic procedure)
  • Non-purulent cellulitis (blood cultures low yield)
  • Respiratory cultures without clinical pneumonia
  • C. diff testing without diarrhea (colonization common)

Blood Culture Guidance

Scenario Recommendation
Suspected bacteremia/sepsis 2 sets (4 bottles) from 2 separate sites before antibiotics
Endocarditis suspected 3 sets from 3 separate sites, 1 hour apart if possible
Follow-up for S. aureus bacteremia Daily or every 48h until negative
Low-grade fever only Usually not indicated without other signs of infection

Procalcitonin-Guided Therapy

Procalcitonin (PCT) can guide antibiotic duration:
  • PCT <0.25: Bacterial infection unlikely - consider stopping antibiotics
  • PCT 0.25-0.5: Bacterial infection possible - clinical judgment
  • PCT >0.5: Bacterial infection likely - continue antibiotics
  • 80% decrease from peak: Consider stopping antibiotics
üíé PCT-guided therapy reduces antibiotic duration in respiratory infections by 2-3 days without affecting outcomes.

üíé Stewardship Pearls

‚ñº
1. Shorter is Better: CAP = 5 days, intra-abdominal = 4 days post source control, cellulitis = 5 days. Longer courses ‚ÜëC. diff and resistance.
2. Beta-lactams > Vancomycin for MSSA: Nafcillin/cefazolin have superior outcomes vs vancomycin for MSSA infections. De-escalate when cultures return.
3. MRSA Nasal Swab: Negative nasal swab has >95% NPV for MRSA pneumonia. Use to discontinue empiric vancomycin/linezolid.
4. Don't Treat Asymptomatic Bacteriuria: Only treat in pregnancy and pre-urologic procedure. Pyuria alone is NOT an indication for treatment.
5. ID Consult for S. aureus Bacteremia: Mandatory - reduces mortality. All SAB needs echo, source evaluation, and duration determination (14 days vs 4-6 weeks).
6. Oral is Often Equivalent: POET/OVIVA trials show oral antibiotics non-inferior to IV for endocarditis and bone infections after initial stabilization.
7. Fluoroquinolone Stewardship: Reserve FQs for when no alternatives exist. Associated with C. diff, tendon rupture, aortic dissection, QTc prolongation.
8. Double Coverage Rarely Needed: Double Pseudomonas or double Gram-negative coverage only indicated for initial empiric therapy in severe sepsis/shock. De-escalate to single agent once susceptibilities return.
9. IV-to-PO Switch Early: Most patients can switch to PO within 48-72h if clinically improving and have functioning GI tract. Linezolid, FQs, metronidazole, TMP-SMX have ~100% bioavailability.
10. Allergy Clarification: 90% of penicillin "allergies" are not true IgE-mediated. Clarify reaction type - many patients can receive beta-lactams.
