Gram+ Cocci

S. aureus (MSSA)

Gram-positive cocci in clusters
Blood / BSIWound / SkinBone / JointRespiratory
Clinical Syndromes
  • Bacteremia / endocarditis
  • Skin & soft tissue (cellulitis, abscess, NecFasc)
  • Osteomyelitis / septic arthritis
  • CAP (post-influenza necrotizing pneumonia)
  • Spinal epidural abscess
First-Line Treatment
Nafcillin/Oxacillin 2g IV q4h OR Cefazolin 2g IV q8h
Alternatives / Allergy
Clindamycin (susceptible); Daptomycin (non-pulmonary); Ceftaroline
Resistance Patterns
MSSA — β-lactam susceptible; de-escalate from vancomycin immediately when confirmed
⚡ Always de-escalate from vancomycin to β-lactam when MSSA confirmed — β-lactam is superior
Gram+ Cocci

MRSA

Gram-positive cocci in clusters
Blood / BSIWound / SkinBone / JointRespiratory
Clinical Syndromes
  • Bacteremia / endocarditis
  • Skin & soft tissue (MRSA most common cause of purulent SSTI)
  • Osteomyelitis / septic arthritis
  • HAP / VAP
  • Spinal epidural abscess
First-Line Treatment
Vancomycin 15–20 mg/kg IV q8–12h (AUC-guided, target 400–600)
Alternatives / Allergy
Daptomycin 6–10 mg/kg IV q24h (not for pneumonia); Linezolid 600mg IV/PO q12h (bacteriostatic); Ceftaroline 600mg IV q8h
Resistance Patterns
mecA → PBP2a; resistant to all β-lactams. Vancomycin preferred. Check for VISA/VRSA if not clearing.
⚡ MRSA nasal swab PCR negative has 95% NPV — can de-escalate MRSA coverage if negative at 48–72h

CoNS (S. epidermidis)

Gram-positive cocci in clusters (smaller colonies than S. aureus)
Blood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • CLABSI (most common cause)
  • Prosthetic valve endocarditis
  • VP shunt / EVD infection
  • Prosthetic joint infection
  • CIED infection
First-Line Treatment
Vancomycin 15–20 mg/kg IV q8–12h (almost always oxacillin-resistant)
Alternatives / Allergy
Daptomycin; Linezolid; Rifampin added for biofilm (device-related)
Resistance Patterns
Usually oxacillin-resistant in healthcare setting — do not use nafcillin
⚡ Single positive blood culture = likely contaminant. Two+ cultures or clinical context required to treat.

Group A Strep (GAS)

Gram-positive cocci in chains
Wound / SkinBlood / BSIRespiratory
Clinical Syndromes
  • Necrotizing fasciitis (Type II — monomicrobial)
  • Streptococcal toxic shock syndrome
  • Cellulitis / erysipelas
  • Pharyngitis
  • Bacteremia
First-Line Treatment
Penicillin G 2–4 MU IV q4–6h + Clindamycin 900mg IV q8h
Alternatives / Allergy
Ampicillin; Cefazolin; Clindamycin alone (if PCN allergy, check susceptibility)

Always penicillin-susceptible. Clindamycin added for toxin inhibition (Eagle effect).

Gram+ Cocci

S. pneumoniae

Gram-positive lancet-shaped diplococci
Blood / BSICSF / CNSRespiratory
Clinical Syndromes
  • CAP (#1 cause of bacterial pneumonia)
  • Bacterial meningitis (#1 adult cause)
  • Bacteremia / sepsis
  • Sinusitis / otitis media
First-Line Treatment
Ceftriaxone 2g IV q12h (CNS) or q24h (non-CNS) + Vancomycin empirically for CNS
Alternatives / Allergy
Penicillin G if susceptible (MIC ≤0.06); Moxifloxacin/Levofloxacin for CAP
Resistance Patterns
PBP mutations — check MIC. Ceftriaxone covers most strains. Add vancomycin for CNS until susceptibilities.
⚡ For meningitis: give dexamethasone before/with first antibiotic dose — reduces mortality in pneumococcal meningitis
Gram+ Cocci

Enterococcus (E. faecalis/faecium)

Gram-positive cocci in pairs/short chains
Urine / UTIBlood / BSIWound / Skin
Clinical Syndromes
  • UTI / CAUTI (very common)
  • Bacteremia / endocarditis
  • Intra-abdominal infections
  • Wound infections
First-Line Treatment
E. faecalis: Ampicillin 2g IV q4h ± Ceftriaxone (synergy for endocarditis); E. faecium: Linezolid or Daptomycin
Alternatives / Allergy
VRE: Linezolid 600mg q12h; Daptomycin 6mg/kg q24h; Tigecycline
Resistance Patterns
E. faecium often ampicillin-resistant and VRE. E. faecalis usually ampicillin-susceptible.
⚡ Enterococcus in urine is often colonization — treat only if symptomatic UTI. In blood cultures, always treat.

Viridans Streptococci

Gram-positive cocci in chains
Blood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Infective endocarditis (native valve, especially dental source)
  • Bacteremia in neutropenic patients
  • Brain abscess (S. anginosus group)

E. coli

Gram-negative rods
Urine / UTIBlood / BSIWound / SkinCSF / CNS
Clinical Syndromes
  • UTI / pyelonephritis (#1 cause)
  • Urosepsis
  • Intra-abdominal infections
  • Biliary infections (cholangitis)
  • Neonatal meningitis
First-Line Treatment
Ceftriaxone 1–2g IV q24h (if susceptible)
Alternatives / Allergy
TMP-SMX PO (if susceptible); Ciprofloxacin (check local resistance); Ertapenem (if ESBL)

  • HAP / VAP

  • Liver abscess (hypervirulent hvKP — especially in diabetics from SE Asia)

Gram− Rods

Pseudomonas

Gram-negative rods (blue-green pigment on culture)
RespiratoryWound / SkinUrine / UTIBlood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • HAP / VAP (most important cause)
  • Bacteremia (high mortality)
  • Wound infections (burn, diabetic foot, surgical)
  • UTI (catheter-associated)
  • Otitis externa / malignant otitis
First-Line Treatment
Cefepime 2g IV q8h OR Meropenem 1–2g IV q8h OR Pip-Tazo 4.5g IV q6h
Alternatives / Allergy
Ciprofloxacin (if susceptible); Aztreonam; Ceftolozane-tazobactam (MDR)
Resistance Patterns
Multiple mechanisms — always check susceptibilities. Two-drug therapy for bacteremia.
⚡ Pip-Tazo MIC creep — for serious infections use cefepime or carbapenem for more reliable coverage
Gram− Coccobacilli

H. influenzae

Gram-negative coccobacilli (pleomorphic)
RespiratoryCSF / CNSBlood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • CAP (non-typeable, especially COPD)
  • Meningitis (type b — unvaccinated)
  • Epiglottitis (type b — now rare with vaccine)
  • Sinusitis / otitis media
  • Bacteremia
First-Line Treatment
Ampicillin-sulbactam 3g IV q6h OR Ceftriaxone 2g IV q24h
Alternatives / Allergy
Amoxicillin-clavulanate PO; Azithromycin; Levofloxacin
Resistance Patterns
β-lactamase production in ~30% — check. Non-β-lactamase ampicillin resistance (BLNAR) emerging.
⚡ Hib vaccine has made type b meningitis and epiglottitis rare — consider in unvaccinated patients
Gram− Diplococci

N. meningitidis

Gram-negative diplococci (intracellular in CSF)
Blood / BSICSF / CNS
Clinical Syndromes
  • Bacterial meningitis (young adults, crowded settings)
  • Meningococcemia / septicemia
  • Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome (adrenal hemorrhage, DIC)
  • Petechial/purpuric rash
First-Line Treatment
Penicillin G 4 MU IV q4h OR Ceftriaxone 2g IV q12h
Alternatives / Allergy
Ampicillin; Meropenem (allergy)
Resistance Patterns
Usually susceptible. Prophylaxis contacts: Ciprofloxacin 500mg PO × 1 OR Rifampin 600mg PO q12h × 2 days
⚡ Chemoprophylaxis for close contacts within 24h. Treat empirically — do not wait for confirmation in suspected meningococcemia

N. gonorrhoeae

Gram-negative diplococci (intracellular in PMNs)
Wound / SkinUrine / UTIBlood / BSICSF / CNS
Clinical Syndromes
  • Urethritis / cervicitis
  • PID
  • Disseminated gonococcal infection (DGI — skin lesions, tenosynovitis, septic arthritis)
  • Pharyngitis / proctitis
  • Ophthalmia neonatorum
First-Line Treatment
Ceftriaxone 500mg IM × 1 (or 1g if weight >150kg); + Doxycycline 100mg PO BID × 7d if chlamydia not excluded

Gram-positive rods (short, may appear coccoid)
Blood / BSICSF / CNS
Clinical Syndromes
  • Meningitis (elderly, pregnant, immunocompromised)
  • Bacteremia

ESBL GNR

Gram-negative rods
Urine / UTIBlood / BSIWound / Skin
Clinical Syndromes
  • UTI / urosepsis (increasing ESBL prevalence)
  • Intra-abdominal infections
  • Bacteremia
  • HAP
First-Line Treatment
Meropenem 1g IV q8h OR Ertapenem 1g IV q24h
Alternatives / Allergy
Ceftazidime-avibactam; Temocillin (Europe); Fosfomycin IV (UTI)

CRE (KPC / NDM / OXA)

Gram-negative rods
Urine / UTIBlood / BSIWound / SkinRespiratory
Clinical Syndromes
  • HAI in ICU / prolonged hospitalization
  • UTI (catheter-associated)
  • Bacteremia (high mortality)
  • Ventilator-associated pneumonia
First-Line Treatment
KPC: Ceftazidime-avibactam 2.5g IV q8h OR Meropenem-vaborbactam; MBL (NDM/VIM): Aztreonam-avibactam OR Cefiderocol

Anaerobe

C. difficile

Gram-positive rod (spore-forming anaerobe)
GI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Antibiotic-associated diarrhea
  • Colitis (pseudomembranous)
  • Fulminant colitis / toxic megacolon
  • Recurrent CDI
First-Line Treatment
Non-severe: Vancomycin 125mg PO QID × 10d OR Fidaxomicin 200mg PO BID × 10d; Fulminant: Vancomycin 500mg PO/NG QID + Metronidazole IV

Fidaxomicin preferred for first recurrence; Bezlotoxumab to prevent recurrence; Fecal transplant (recurrent)

Fungus

Candida spp.

Yeast (budding) + pseudohyphae on culture
Blood / BSIUrine / UTIWound / Skin
Clinical Syndromes
  • Candidemia (ICU, immunocompromised — 40–60% mortality)
  • Invasive candidiasis (hepatosplenic, peritonitis)
  • CAUTI (often colonization)
  • Esophagitis (HIV/immunocompromised)
  • Endophthalmitis
First-Line Treatment
Caspofungin 70mg load → 50mg IV q24h OR Micafungin 100mg IV q24h; Step-down: Fluconazole 400mg daily (susceptible species)
Alternatives / Allergy
Anidulafungin; Liposomal amphotericin (neutropenic/azole-resistant); Rezafungin
Resistance Patterns
C. glabrata: reduced fluconazole susceptibility — use echinocandin. C. auris: pan-resistant possible — contact ID + infection control. C. parapsilosis: reduced echinocandin susceptibility.
⚡ Echinocandins have poor ocular penetration — use fluconazole/voriconazole for Candida endophthalmitis

Septate hyphae at 45° angles on histology

Clinical Syndromes
  • Invasive pulmonary aspergillosis (IPA — neutropenic, transplant)
  • Tracheobronchitis (lung transplant)
  • Cerebral aspergillosis
  • Sinusitis

Voriconazole 6mg/kg IV q12h × 2 doses → 4mg/kg IV q12h (first-line); OR Isavuconazole

Fungus

Cryptococcus

Encapsulated yeast (India ink — clear halo)
CSF / CNSBlood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Meningitis (HIV/immunocompromised — subacute onset)
  • Pulmonary cryptococcosis
  • Fungemia (disseminated)
First-Line Treatment
Induction: Liposomal amphotericin B 3–4mg/kg IV q24h + Flucytosine 25mg/kg PO QID × 2 weeks; Consolidation: Fluconazole 400mg PO × 8 weeks; Maintenance: Fluconazole 200mg PO lifelong (HIV)
Alternatives / Allergy
Fluconazole alone for non-CNS pulmonary in low-risk
Resistance Patterns
Usually susceptible. Check serum/CSF cryptococcal antigen (CrAg) — highly sensitive/specific.
⚡ ICP management critical — serial LPs or CSF drainage for elevated pressure; ICP kills more than the fungus

PCP (Pneumocystis)

Cysts and trophic forms on BAL (GMS stain)
Respiratory
Clinical Syndromes
  • Pneumocystis pneumonia (PCP) — HIV CD4 <200, immunocompromised
First-Line Treatment
TMP-SMX 15–20 mg/kg/day IV divided q6–8h × 21 days; Add Prednisone 40mg PO BID × 5d if PaO2 <70 or A-a gradient >35

Myco / AFB

M. tuberculosis (TB)

Acid-fast bacilli (AFB) on Ziehl-Neelsen stain
RespiratoryCSF / CNSBone / JointBlood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Pulmonary TB (cavitary, upper lobe)
  • TB meningitis
  • Miliary TB (disseminated)
  • Vertebral osteomyelitis (Pott disease)
  • Pericarditis / pleural effusion
  • Latent TB (LTBI)
First-Line Treatment
RIPE: Rifampin + Isoniazid + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol × 2 months; then Rifampin + Isoniazid × 4 months
Alternatives / Allergy
Drug-resistant TB: fluoroquinolone-based regimens; consult ID/pulmonology
Resistance Patterns
MDR-TB: resistant to INH + RIF. XDR-TB: also resistant to fluoroquinolones. Susceptibility testing mandatory.
⚡ Airborne isolation mandatory. LTBI: INH × 9 months OR Rifampin × 4 months OR 3HP (rifapentine + INH weekly × 12 doses)

MAC (M. avium)

Acid-fast bacilli
Blood / BSIRespiratory
Clinical Syndromes
  • Disseminated MAC (HIV CD4 <50)
  • Pulmonary MAC (non-HIV, elderly women — Lady Windermere syndrome)
  • Lymphadenitis (children)

Influenza

Orthomyxovirus — diagnosed by rapid antigen or PCR
Respiratory
Clinical Syndromes
  • Influenza syndrome (fever, myalgia, cough)
  • Influenza pneumonia
  • Secondary bacterial pneumonia (S. aureus, S. pneumoniae, S. pyogenes)
First-Line Treatment
Oseltamivir 75mg PO BID × 5 days (start within 48h); Hospitalized/severe: treat regardless of duration
Alternatives / Allergy
Baloxavir 40–80mg PO × 1 dose; Zanamivir inhaled; Peramivir IV
Resistance Patterns

HSV-1 / HSV-2

Enveloped DNA virus — diagnosed by PCR or DFA
CSF / CNSWound / SkinBlood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • HSV encephalitis (temporal lobe — HSV-1)
  • Genital herpes (HSV-2)
  • Mucocutaneous lesions (oral, genital, perianal)
  • Neonatal HSV (disseminated — high mortality)
  • HSV keratitis

Encephalitis: Acyclovir 10mg/kg IV q8h × 14–21 days; Genital/mucocutaneous: Valacyclovir 1g PO BID × 7–10d

CMV

Enveloped DNA herpesvirus — owl-eye inclusions on histology
Blood / BSIGIRespiratory
Clinical Syndromes
  • CMV disease in transplant (colitis, pneumonitis, retinitis, hepatitis)
  • CMV mononucleosis (immunocompetent)
  • Retinitis (HIV CD4 <50)
  • CMV viremia (transplant monitoring)
First-Line Treatment

Parasite

Malaria (Plasmodium)

Intraerythrocytic rings on thick/thin smear
Blood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Uncomplicated malaria (fever, chills, rigors)
  • Severe malaria (P. falciparum — cerebral, ARDS, renal failure, hyperparasitemia)
First-Line Treatment
P. falciparum: Artemether-lumefantrine (Coartem) OR Atovaquone-proguanil; Severe: IV Artesunate; P. vivax/ovale: Chloroquine + Primaquine (eradicate hypnozoites)
Alternatives / Allergy
Quinine + doxycycline; Mefloquine
Resistance Patterns
P. falciparum chloroquine-resistant worldwide. Artemisinin partial resistance (SE Asia). IV artesunate for severe — superior to quinine.
⚡ Thick and thin smears × 3 at 12–24h intervals if initial negative. Quantify % parasitemia.

  • CNS toxoplasmosis (HIV CD4 <100 — multiple ring-enhancing lesions)
