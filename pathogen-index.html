Flavivirus (4 serotypes) — NS1 antigen or PCR (early), serology (late)
Blood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Dengue fever: abrupt fever, severe headache, retro-orbital pain, myalgia, bone pain ('breakbone fever')
  • Dengue hemorrhagic fever / severe dengue: plasma leakage, thrombocytopenia, hemorrhage
  • Caribbean, Puerto Rico, South Florida, travel
  • Second infection with different serotype = highest severe risk (ADE)
First-Line Treatment
Supportive — IV fluids, acetaminophen (NO NSAIDs/aspirin — bleeding risk); no specific antivirals
Alternatives / Allergy
N/A
Resistance Patterns
N/A
⚡ Thrombocytopenia + normal or low WBC + travel to tropics = dengue until proven otherwise. NSAIDs contraindicated.

  • Mild: low-grade fever, maculopapular rash, arthralgia, non-purulent conjunctivitis

Supportive — acetaminophen; GBS: IVIG or plasmapheresis; no antivirals

Hantavirus (HPS)

Bunyavirus — serology or PCR
RespiratoryBlood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Hantavirus pulmonary syndrome (HPS): prodrome → rapid ARDS
  • Deer mouse (Peromyscus) reservoir — inhalation of rodent droppings/urine
  • Southwest US, Four Corners region; also Midwest, Southeast
  • 30–40% case fatality

Supportive ICU — ECMO for severe cases; no proven antivirals (IV ribavirin not proven effective)
Alternatives / Allergy
ECMO for refractory hypoxemia
Resistance Patterns
N/A
⚡ Prodrome mimics flu, then sudden respiratory failure. Rodent exposure + Southwest US + bilateral infiltrates = HPS.

Mpox (Monkeypox)

Orthopoxvirus — PCR on lesion for diagnosis
Wound / SkinBlood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Rash: begins face/genitals → spreads; deep-seated, umbilicated lesions all in same stage
  • Lymphadenopathy (distinguishes from smallpox)
  • Proctitis, pharyngitis (MSM outbreak 2022)
  • Systemic: fever, headache, myalgia
  • Severe in immunocompromised
First-Line Treatment
Mild: supportive; Severe/immunocompromised: Tecovirimat (TPOXX) 600mg PO BID × 14d (IND protocol)
Alternatives / Allergy
Brincidofovir; Cidofovir; VIGIV (vaccinia immune globulin)
Resistance Patterns
Tecovirimat resistance emerging in heavily treated patients.
⚡ Contact and droplet precautions (airborne if lesions on respiratory mucosa). JYNNEOS vaccine for post-exposure prophylaxis within 4 days.

Histoplasma

Dimorphic fungus — small yeast inside macrophages (tissue); mold in environment
RespiratoryBlood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Acute pulmonary (bird/bat droppings, Ohio/Mississippi River Valley)
  • Disseminated histoplasmosis (HIV CD4 <150, immunocompromised)
  • Chronic cavitary (COPD)
  • Mediastinal granuloma / fibrosing mediastinitis
First-Line Treatment
Mild-mod: Itraconazole 200mg PO TID × 3d → BID × 12 weeks; Severe/disseminated: Liposomal amphotericin B 3mg/kg × 1–2 weeks → Itraconazole × 12 months
Alternatives / Allergy
Voriconazole; Posaconazole (azole-intolerant)
Resistance Patterns
Check itraconazole levels (target trough >1 mcg/mL). Urine/serum Histoplasma antigen for diagnosis.
⚡ Urine Histoplasma antigen most sensitive for disseminated disease. Skin test not useful clinically.

Coccidioides (Valley Fever)

Dimorphic — spherules with endospores in tissue; arthroconidia in environment (NEVER culture outside biosafety cabinet)
RespiratoryCSF / CNSBlood / BSIBone / Joint
Clinical Syndromes
  • Valley fever: flu-like illness (Southwest US, San Joaquin Valley)
  • Erythema nodosum (immune response — good prognostic sign)
  • Disseminated: skin, bone, meningitis (can be fatal without lifelong treatment)
  • Highest risk: Black, Filipino, pregnancy, immunocompromised
First-Line Treatment
Mild pulmonary: observation or Fluconazole 400mg PO daily × 3–6 months; Severe/disseminated: Fluconazole 400–800mg PO daily; Meningitis: Fluconazole lifelong; ARDS: Liposomal amphotericin B
Alternatives / Allergy
Itraconazole; Voriconazole; Posaconazole; Amphotericin (severe)
Resistance Patterns
Meningitis requires lifelong fluconazole — stopping causes relapse.
⚡ Alert lab — spherules are infectious. Serology (IgM/IgG) most useful diagnostically; CF titer >1:16 = disseminated risk.

Blastomyces (Blastomycosis)

Dimorphic — broad-based budding yeast (tissue); mold in environment
RespiratoryWound / SkinBone / JointCSF / CNS
Clinical Syndromes
  • Pulmonary blastomycosis (Great Lakes, Ohio/Mississippi Valley, Southeast US)
  • Skin (verrucous/ulcerative lesions)
  • Bone (osteomyelitis)
  • CNS (rare but severe)
  • Mimics malignancy, TB
First-Line Treatment
Mild-mod: Itraconazole 200mg PO TID × 3d → BID × 6–12 months; Severe/CNS: Liposomal amphotericin B → Itraconazole
Alternatives / Allergy
Voriconazole (CNS penetration); Posaconazole
Resistance Patterns
Check itraconazole levels. Urine Blastomyces antigen available.
⚡ Does NOT cluster in bird/bat droppings like Histoplasma. Soil, waterways. Skin lesions with pulmonary = biopsy for diagnosis.

Sporothrix (Sporotrichosis)

Dimorphic — cigar-shaped yeast in tissue; mold with rosette conidia in culture
Wound / Skin
Clinical Syndromes
  • Lymphocutaneous: primary nodule → ulceration → ascending lymphangitis (classic)
  • Fixed cutaneous (plaque)
  • Disseminated (immunocompromised)
  • Rose thorn prick, sphagnum moss, bark, cat scratch (zoonotic sporotrichosis — Brazil)
First-Line Treatment
Lymphocutaneous: Itraconazole 200mg PO daily × 3–6 months; Disseminated: Liposomal amphotericin B → Itraconazole
Alternatives / Allergy
Potassium iodide (historical, PO); Terbinafine
Resistance Patterns
Fluconazole inferior — do not use. Itraconazole is first-line.
⚡ Painless nodule tracking up the arm after thorn/soil exposure = sporotrichosis until proven otherwise

Mucormycetes (Mucormycosis)

Non-septate (aseptate) ribbon-like hyphae at 90° angles (tissue)
RespiratoryCSF / CNSWound / Skin
Clinical Syndromes
  • Rhinoorbital-cerebral (DKA, immunocompromised — palate necrosis, proptosis)
  • Pulmonary (neutropenic, transplant)
  • Cutaneous (trauma, burns)
  • GI, disseminated (rare)
  • COVID-19 associated mucormycosis (India — corticosteroids + DM)
First-Line Treatment
Liposomal amphotericin B 5–10mg/kg IV q24h; Surgical debridement mandatory; Control underlying (reverse DKA, reduce immunosuppression)
Alternatives / Allergy
Isavuconazole (FDA approved); Posaconazole (step-down); combination (limited evidence)
Resistance Patterns
Intrinsically resistant to voriconazole — do NOT use for Mucor.
⚡ Voriconazole does NOT cover Mucor — Aspergillus coverage ≠ Mucor coverage. Liposomal amphotericin + surgery.

  • Wound infections

Entamoeba histolytica (Amebiasis)

Trophozoites with ingested RBCs (pathognomonic); cysts in stool
GIBlood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Amebic colitis (bloody diarrhea, cramping, tenesmus)
  • Amebic liver abscess (RUQ pain, fever, elevated ALP — single right lobe cavity)
  • Travel to endemic areas, MSM, institutionalized
First-Line Treatment
Tissue active: Metronidazole 750mg PO/IV TID × 7–10d; THEN luminal agent: Paromomycin 500mg PO TID × 7d
Alternatives / Allergy
Tinidazole (tissue); Iodoquinol (luminal)
Resistance Patterns
Luminal agent mandatory after metronidazole — eliminates gut cysts, prevents relapse.
⚡ Serology positive in >90% of liver abscess — more useful than stool O&P. Drainage usually NOT needed (responds to metronidazole).

Giardia

Pear-shaped trophozoites with 2 nuclei (owl face); cysts in stool
GI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Watery diarrhea, bloating, flatulence, steatorrhea (malabsorption)
  • Campers/hikers (contaminated water — beavers = 'beaver fever')
  • Daycare centers, international travel
  • Chronic infection in immunocompromised (especially hypogammaglobulinemia)
First-Line Treatment
Metronidazole 250mg PO TID × 5–7d OR Tinidazole 2g PO × 1
Alternatives / Allergy
Nitazoxanide; Paromomycin (pregnancy — non-absorbable)
Resistance Patterns
Metronidazole resistance possible — tinidazole or combination therapy.
⚡ Stool antigen EIA most sensitive (>90%). O&P microscopy less sensitive — requires 3 specimens.

Cryptosporidium

Oocysts on modified AFB stain or antigen test
GI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Watery diarrhea (immunocompetent: self-limited 1–2 weeks)
  • Severe chronic diarrhea and wasting (HIV CD4 <100 — cryptosporidiosis)
  • Waterborne outbreaks (chlorine-resistant oocysts)
  • Contact with calves/livestock
First-Line Treatment
Immunocompetent: Nitazoxanide 500mg PO BID × 3d; HIV: ART + Nitazoxanide (immune restoration most important)
Alternatives / Allergy
Paromomycin (limited efficacy); Supportive fluids
Resistance Patterns
Chlorine-resistant — use UV or boiling for water purification.
⚡ Standard water filtration removes oocysts. Milwaukee 1993 outbreak — 403,000 people affected from municipal water.

Strongyloides

Rhabditiform larvae in stool; filariform larvae in tissue
GIBlood / BSIRespiratory
Clinical Syndromes
  • Chronic intestinal (often asymptomatic — eosinophilia)
  • Cutaneous larva currens (serpiginous urticarial rash)
  • Hyperinfection syndrome (steroids/immunosuppression) — larvae disseminate carrying gut bacteria → gram-negative sepsis/meningitis
  • High mortality if untreated hyperinfection
First-Line Treatment
Ivermectin 200 mcg/kg PO × 2 days (repeat in 2 weeks); Hyperinfection: daily ivermectin until larval clearance
Alternatives / Allergy
Albendazole 400mg PO BID × 7d (less effective)
Resistance Patterns
Screen all patients from endemic areas before starting steroids/immunosuppression.
⚡ Eosinophilia + tropical exposure + starting steroids = screen for Strongyloides first. Hyperinfection has 60–80% mortality.

Trypanosoma cruzi (Chagas)

Trypomastigotes in blood smear (acute); serology for chronic
Blood / BSI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Acute: Romaña sign (unilateral painless periorbital swelling), chagoma, fever
  • Chronic cardiac: cardiomyopathy, arrhythmia, sudden death
  • Chronic GI: megaesophagus, megacolon
  • Reduviid (kissing) bug — Latin America, South Texas, imported cases
  • Reactivation in immunocompromised (meningoencephalitis)
First-Line Treatment
Benznidazole 5–7mg/kg/day PO × 60d (preferred); Nifurtimox 8–10mg/kg/day PO × 90d
Alternatives / Allergy
Both drugs available through CDC
Resistance Patterns
Treatment most effective in acute phase. Chronic cardiac disease: manage heart failure/arrhythmia.
⚡ Screen blood donors and transplant donors/recipients from endemic areas. Test all patients from Latin America with cardiomyopathy.

Naegleria fowleri (PAM)

Free-living amoeba — CSF wet prep shows motile trophozoites
CSF / CNS
Clinical Syndromes
  • Primary amebic meningoencephalitis (PAM) — rapidly fatal
  • Young, healthy swimmers in warm freshwater (lakes, rivers, South US)
  • Severe headache, fever, nausea → altered mental status → coma → death (1–18 days)
  • Mortality >97%
First-Line Treatment
Miltefosine (CDC Emergency IND) + Amphotericin B + Azithromycin + Fluconazole; therapeutic hypothermia
Alternatives / Allergy
Combination protocol — few survivors
Resistance Patterns
N/A — nearly universally fatal
⚡ Warm freshwater + young swimmer + rapidly progressive meningoencephalitis = send CSF for Naegleria immediately. Contact CDC.

Schistosoma (Schistosomiasis)

Eggs with characteristic spines in stool/urine; serology
Blood / BSIGIUrine / UTI
Clinical Syndromes
  • Katayama fever (acute — fever, eosinophilia, urticaria — weeks after freshwater exposure)
  • Hepatic schistosomiasis: portal hypertension, varices (S. mansoni — Caribbean, Africa)
  • Urogenital (S. haematobium — hematuria, bladder carcinoma)
  • Neuroschistosomiasis (rare — seizures, myelopathy)
First-Line Treatment
Praziquantel 40mg/kg/day PO × 1 day (2 doses 4h apart); Katayama: Corticosteroids + Praziquantel
Alternatives / Allergy
Oxamniquine (S. mansoni — limited availability)
Resistance Patterns
Praziquantel resistance emerging in some regions.
⚡ Caribbean freshwater contact (especially Puerto Rico, Martinique) + eosinophilia = screen for S. mansoni. Single dose praziquantel is highly effective.
