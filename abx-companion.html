Pearls
  • Narrow-spectrum when possible — broad-spectrum empiric coverage is a bridging strategy, not a destination; always de-escalate
  • Spectrum ≠ potency — a drug active against a pathogen at the right site is superior to a broader drug with poor penetration
  • Local antibiogram matters — resistance rates for E. coli TMP-SMX, FQ, and cephalosporins vary dramatically by institution and region; check annually
  • Never use Vancomycin for MSSA — beta-lactams are superior; Vanc is a fallback, not an upgrade

Lung — ELF
(Epithelial Lining Fluid)		 Azithromycin (ELF >100× serum — intracellular accumulation), Linezolid (ELF >serum), Fluoroquinolones (ELF ~1× serum), TMP-SMX Ceftriaxone, Pip-tazo, Meropenem (ELF ~30–50% serum) Daptomycin (inactivated by surfactant — absolute contraindication), Vancomycin (ELF ~25% serum — subtherapeutic for MRSA pneumonia) Linezolid superior to Vancomycin for MRSA pneumonia (ZEPHyR trial) due to ELF concentration advantage. Azithromycin tissue concentrations persist days after last dose. Never use Daptomycin for any pulmonary infection.
Intracellular
(Macrophages, Phagocytes)		 Azithromycin / Macrolides (concentrated 10–100×), Fluoroquinolones (2–10×), Doxycycline (good), TMP-SMX (good), Rifampin (excellent), Linezolid Clindamycin (moderate) Beta-lactams (poor intracellular penetration — no activity against intracellular pathogens), Vancomycin, Aminoglycosides Essential for atypicals (Legionella, Mycoplasma, Chlamydophila), Brucella, Salmonella, Listeria, Toxoplasma, MAC. Explains why beta-lactam monotherapy fails for Legionella and why macrolides/FQs are mandatory for atypical CAP coverage.
Urine Nitrofurantoin (urine only — no systemic activity), Fosfomycin (high urinary concentration), TMP-SMX (high urinary levels), Fluoroquinolones, most beta-lactams (renally excreted) Ceftriaxone, Gentamicin Doxycycline (poor urinary levels), Azithromycin (minimal urinary excretion), Clindamycin Nitrofurantoin and Fosfomycin are NOT suitable for pyelonephritis or systemic infection — activity confined to the bladder. Fluoroquinolone urinary levels far exceed serum MIC — explains 3-day efficacy for uncomplicated UTI.
Prostate Fluoroquinolones (Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin — excellent lipid solubility, 30–50% tissue levels), TMP-SMX (good), Doxycycline Azithromycin (moderate) Most beta-lactams (poor lipid solubility, low tissue levels), Aminoglycosides, Vancomycin, Nitrofurantoin Fluoroquinolones are first-line for bacterial prostatitis for this reason. Beta-lactams fail despite adequate urinary levels — poor stromal penetration. Minimum 4–6 weeks required for chronic prostatitis regardless of agent.
Biofilm
(Hardware / Implant)		 Rifampin (biofilm penetration — always combine, never monotherapy), Fluoroquinolones (relative biofilm activity), Daptomycin (high-dose) Linezolid, TMP-SMX, Fosfomycin (combination) Beta-lactams (minimal biofilm activity alone), Vancomycin (poor biofilm penetration despite MRSA activity), Aminoglycosides Biofilm increases MIC 100–1000×. Hardware removal is definitive treatment — antibiotics suppress but rarely eradicate biofilm infection. Rifampin must always be combined (monotherapy selects resistance in days). DAIR (débridement, antibiotics, implant retention) protocol requires Rifampin combination.
Abscess Cavity Metronidazole (excellent penetration + anaerobic activity), Fluoroquinolones, TMP-SMX, Linezolid, Clindamycin Beta-lactams (penetrate but reduced activity in low pH/anaerobic environment) Aminoglycosides (inactivated at low pH, poor anaerobic penetration), Vancomycin Drainage is mandatory — antibiotics cannot penetrate necrotic core. Aminoglycosides lose activity in acidic abscess environment regardless of systemic levels. Most agents require drainage for clinical cure.
Vitreous / Aqueous
(Endophthalmitis)		 Fluoroquinolones (Moxifloxacin — best ocular penetration of all systemic agents), Voriconazole (fungal), Linezolid Vancomycin (intravitreal preferred), TMP-SMX Most beta-lactams, Aminoglycosides (systemic dosing inadequate — require intravitreal) Systemic antibiotics rarely achieve therapeutic vitreous levels — intravitreal injection (Vancomycin 1 mg + Ceftazidime 2.25 mg) is standard for bacterial endophthalmitis. Moxifloxacin is exception with adequate systemic penetration. Voriconazole PO achieves therapeutic vitreous levels for fungal endophthalmitis.
Cardiac Vegetations
(Endocarditis)		 Bactericidal agents required: Penicillin G, Nafcillin, Cefazolin (MSSA); Vancomycin, Daptomycin (MRSA); Ampicillin + Gentamicin (Enterococcus) Ceftriaxone (viridans strep) Bacteriostatic agents (Linezolid, Tetracyclines, Clindamycin) — avoid as monotherapy; vegetation penetration less relevant than bactericidal requirement Vegetations are high-inoculum, protein-rich fibrin matrices. Bactericidal activity mandatory — bacteriostatic agents associated with relapse. Duration 4–6 weeks minimum to sterilize. Aminoglycoside synergy shortens duration for susceptible streptococci.

Pearls
  • Tissue level ≠ serum level — many agents with excellent serum concentrations fail at specific sites (Vancomycin in lung, aminoglycosides in abscess, beta-lactams intracellularly)
  • Inflammation opens barriers — meningeal, blood-brain, and blood-prostate barriers become more permeable with active infection; this is why some agents work in acute but not chronic infection
  • Daptomycin + lung = never — pulmonary surfactant irreversibly inactivates Daptomycin; this applies to any pulmonary infection regardless of pathogen susceptibility
  • Biofilm demands hardware removal — no antibiotic reliably sterilizes a mature biofilm; Rifampin combination is the best pharmacologic adjunct but is not a substitute for source control
  • Urine concentration ≠ tissue penetration — Nitrofurantoin has excellent bladder levels but zero kidney or systemic activity; never use for pyelonephritis
  • Intracellular pathogens need intracellular antibiotics — Legionella, Mycoplasma, Brucella, Chlamydophila, Salmonella, and MAC all survive inside phagocytes; beta-lactams never reach them

IV-to-PO Switch — Criteria & High-Bioavailability Agents
▼
Standard Switch Criteria (all must be met)
  • Tolerating oral intake (no vomiting, functioning GI tract)
  • Clinical improvement — afebrile ≥24h, hemodynamically stable, improving WBC/inflammatory markers
  • Oral agent available with appropriate spectrum and adequate bioavailability for the infection site
  • No CNS infection, endocarditis, or S. aureus bacteremia (exceptions: OVIVA trial data supports PO for bone/joint infections)

Antimicrobial Stewardship — Core Principles
▼
Key Stewardship Interventions
  • Prospective audit and feedback — ID pharmacist / ID physician review of broad-spectrum antibiotic prescriptions at 48–72h; evidence strongest for reducing inappropriate use
  • Pre-authorization / formulary restriction — carbapenems, anti-MRSA agents, antifungals requiring approval before dispensing; reduces consumption without adverse outcomes
  • IV-to-PO conversion — automatic switch protocols for eligible high-bioavailability agents reduce hospital days without increased failure rates
  • Diagnostic stewardship — avoid ordering urine cultures in asymptomatic patients; use procalcitonin to guide antibiotic duration and de-escalation
  • Duration bundles — predefined treatment durations for common syndromes (CAP 5d, uncomplicated UTI 3–5d, uncomplicated pyelonephritis 7d, SSTI 5d) reduce unnecessary prolonged courses

Procalcitonin-Guided Therapy
PCT <0.1 ng/mL: bacterial infection unlikely — withhold antibiotics in low-risk respiratory infections · PCT <0.25 ng/mL: consider stopping · PCT fall >80% from peak: supports de-escalation / discontinuation · PCT-guided protocols reduce antibiotic duration by 2–3 days in CAP and sepsis without increased mortality (PRORATA, PASS trials).

Pearls & Pitfalls
  • Remove central venous catheter as soon as feasible in all candidemia — source control is critical and reduces duration of BSI
  • Ophthalmology exam within 7 days — endophthalmitis occurs in 5–10% of candidemia; may require intravitreal antifungal
  • Echocardiography for persistent candidemia (>72h positive cultures after initiation of therapy)
  • Follow-up blood cultures every 24–48h until cleared — minimum 14 days from first negative culture, regardless of clinical improvement
  • β-d-glucan elevated in most invasive fungal infections — useful adjunct but not species-specific; not useful for mucormycosis

Aspergillus — Invasive Pulmonary Aspergillosis (IPA)
▼
Risk Factors & Diagnosis
Prolonged neutropenia (>10 days) — highest risk Hematopoietic stem cell transplant Solid organ transplant (lung highest) High-dose corticosteroids (>3 weeks) CGD / inherited immunodeficiency
Preferred Therapy
Voriconazole 6 mg/kg IV q12h × 2 doses (load) → 4 mg/kg IV q12h → PO step-down (200 mg PO q12h)
TDM: target trough 1–5.5 µg/mL; <1 = subtherapeutic; >5.5 = neurotoxicity risk
≥6–12 wk (or until immune reconstitution)
Alternative / Voriconazole-intolerant:
Isavuconazole 372 mg (isavuconazonium) IV/PO q8h × 6 doses → 372 mg q24h (better tolerability, fewer drug interactions)
or Liposomal Amphotericin B 3–5 mg/kg IV q24h (salvage / voriconazole failure)
≥6–12 wk
Pearls & Pitfalls
  • Galactomannan assay (serum or BAL) — sensitivity 70–80% in neutropenic IPA; low sensitivity in non-neutropenic/solid organ transplant patients
  • CT chest halo sign (nodule surrounded by ground-glass) — early IPA in neutropenic patients; air-crescent sign = later finding (recovering neutrophils)
  • Voriconazole CYP2C19 polymorphism — extensive metabolizers have lower troughs; Asian populations more likely to be poor metabolizers with toxicity risk; TDM is mandatory
  • Azole-resistant Aspergillus (TR34/L98H mutation in fumigatus) emerging in Europe and Asia — check susceptibility if endemic region or prior azole exposure
  • Combination therapy (Voriconazole + Echinocandin) — not routinely superior; consider for salvage therapy in refractory IPA (IDSA 2016)

ICP Management — Critical
Elevated ICP (>25 cmH₂O) — major cause of early mortality. Therapeutic LP to reduce opening pressure to ≤20 cmH₂O; daily LPs until pressure controlled. Do NOT use corticosteroids for ICP management (worsens outcomes). Lumbar drain or VP shunt for refractory ICP.
Pearls & Pitfalls
  • Serum CrAg (cryptococcal antigen) — sensitivity >99% for disseminated disease; test all HIV patients with CD4 <100 (CrAg screen and pre-emptive treatment)
  • 5-FC monitoring: check levels / CBC every 72–96h — bone marrow suppression, GI toxicity; must be dose-adjusted for renal dysfunction
  • Immune reconstitution inflammatory syndrome (IRIS): paradoxical worsening 4–8 weeks after starting ART — manage with corticosteroids (unlike ICP management)
  • Defer ART for 4–6 weeks after starting antifungal therapy to reduce IRIS risk (COAT trial)

Section A · By Drug Class
Vancomycin (Glycopeptide)
▼
  • AUC/MIC-guided dosing is now standard — trough-only monitoring is obsolete. Target AUC₂₄ 400–600 mg·h/L. Trough-only monitoring overestimates nephrotoxicity risk and underestimates efficacy failure. ASHP/IDSA/SIDP 2020 PMID 32207637
  • Never use Vancomycin as definitive therapy for MSSA — beta-lactams are superior. Multiple studies and meta-analyses demonstrate higher clinical failure rates with Vancomycin for MSSA bacteremia vs. antistaphylococcal penicillins or cefazolin. CID 2011 PMID 21810755
  • Vancomycin penetration into the lung is poor relative to achievable serum concentrations — for MRSA pneumonia, Linezolid achieves superior ELF concentrations. Consider Linezolid as preferred agent for MRSA VAP/HAP when appropriate. IDSA/ATS HAP 2016
  • Nephrotoxicity risk is synergistic with piperacillin-tazobactam — the Vanc + Pip-Tazo combination carries significantly higher AKI rates than Vanc + carbapenem or Vanc monotherapy. Use with awareness; consider substituting Cefepime or Meropenem when combination is required. PMID 27694236
  • Oral Vancomycin is not absorbed systemically — active only intraluminally. Do not use for systemic infections. High-dose oral Vancomycin (500 mg QID) can be used for fulminant CDI when enteric access is preserved.
  • Red Man Syndrome is infusion-rate dependent, not a true allergy — slow the infusion rate; premedicate with diphenhydramine. Do not document as "Vancomycin allergy." Distinguish from true IgE-mediated hypersensitivity (rare).
Drug Guide: Vancomycin S. aureus BSI card HAP/VAP card
Daptomycin (Lipopeptide)
▼
  • Daptomycin is inactivated by pulmonary surfactant — it is absolutely contraindicated for pneumonia of any etiology. Do not use for MRSA pneumonia. This is one of the most critical drug-class pitfalls in ID. PMID 15150609
  • Dose-escalate for bacteremia and endocarditis — standard 6 mg/kg/day is appropriate for skin/soft tissue infections. For S. aureus bacteremia and right-sided endocarditis, use ≥6 mg/kg; for left-sided endocarditis, ≥8–10 mg/kg. PMID 16195521
  • Monitor CK weekly — myopathy/rhabdomyolysis risk, especially with concurrent statins. Discontinue statins if able during Daptomycin therapy. Hold if CK >5× ULN.
  • Daptomycin MIC creep in MRSA — prior Vancomycin exposure can reduce Daptomycin susceptibility (heteroresistance). If MIC rising or clinical failure, consider high-dose Daptomycin ± beta-lactam combination (Daptomycin + ceftaroline synergy is documented). PMID 25182998
  • Concentration-dependent killing — single daily dosing is pharmacodynamically optimal. No benefit to divided dosing.
Drug Guide: Daptomycin S. aureus BSI card IE Native Valve card
Linezolid (Oxazolidinone)
▼
  • Serotonin syndrome with SSRIs/SNRIs/TCAs — Linezolid is a reversible MAO inhibitor. Potentially fatal serotonin toxicity when combined with serotonergic agents. Requires careful risk-benefit assessment; consider stopping serotonergic drug if clinical urgency demands Linezolid. FDA Safety Communication
  • Linezolid is preferred over Vancomycin for MRSA pneumonia — superior epithelial lining fluid concentrations; ZEPHyR trial demonstrated non-inferiority with trend toward superiority for clinical cure in MRSA VAP. ZEPHyR Trial PMID 22506023
  • Thrombocytopenia is the most common serious adverse effect — occurs in ~3% overall; higher with courses >2 weeks and in renal impairment. Check CBC weekly. Discontinue if platelet count falls significantly.
  • Near-complete oral bioavailability (~100%) — IV-to-PO switch can be made immediately in clinically stable patients with functioning GI tract. Oral Linezolid achieves identical serum levels to IV. PMID 10227160
  • Bacteriostatic, not bactericidal — caution in endocarditis and CNS infections where bactericidal activity is generally preferred. Not first-line for endocarditis; reserve as salvage option.
  • Lactic acidosis — rare but life-threatening with prolonged use (>28 days). Presents with nausea, abdominal pain, elevated lactate. Monitor lactate in long courses.
Drug Guide: Linezolid HAP/VAP card
Fluoroquinolones
▼
  • Fluoroquinolones are broad-spectrum collateral damage agents — reserve for serious infections where no reasonable alternative exists. Avoid for uncomplicated UTI, upper respiratory infections, and routine sinusitis. Overuse drives Clostridioides difficile, MRSA, and resistant GNR selection. IDSA UTI 2010
  • Pseudomonas monotherapy resistance emergence is rapid — for serious Pseudomonas infections, Ciprofloxacin or Levofloxacin monotherapy is associated with emergence of resistance on-therapy. Combination therapy or high-dose regimens should be used for high-inoculum infections. PMID 16195524
  • QTc prolongation — additive with other QT-prolonging drugs — obtain baseline ECG and avoid combining with other QTc prolongers (antipsychotics, azithromycin, amiodarone). Moxifloxacin has the highest QTc risk among quinolones.
  • Tendinopathy and tendon rupture — Achilles most common. Risk increased with age >60, corticosteroids, renal failure. FDA Black Box Warning. Counsel patients to stop and report tendon pain immediately.
  • Neurotoxicity and aortic aneurysm/dissection risk — FDA Black Box Warnings (2016, 2018). Peripheral neuropathy may be permanent. Association with aortic dissection in population-based studies — avoid in patients with known aortic disease.
  • Ciprofloxacin is NOT reliable for Streptococcal or pneumococcal infections — do not use for CAP empiric therapy unless you have documented susceptibility. Levofloxacin and Moxifloxacin have adequate pneumococcal activity; Ciprofloxacin does not.
  • Excellent oral bioavailability (~70–99%) — strong IV-to-PO switch candidates for Ciprofloxacin (70–80%), Levofloxacin (~99%), and Moxifloxacin (~90%).
Drug Guide: Cipro Drug Guide: Levofloxacin CAP card UTI card
Aminoglycosides
▼
  • Extended-interval (once-daily) dosing is preferred for systemic infections — exploits concentration-dependent killing and post-antibiotic effect; demonstrated equal or superior efficacy with less nephrotoxicity vs. traditional divided dosing. Hartford nomogram or trough-based monitoring. PMID 8512153
  • Traditional divided dosing retained for synergy (endocarditis) — Gentamicin 3 mg/kg/day ÷ q8h for Enterococcal synergy and for streptococcal endocarditis short-course regimens. Extended-interval dosing is NOT appropriate for synergy indications.
  • Nephrotoxicity is largely reversible; ototoxicity is not — cochlear toxicity (high-frequency hearing loss) and vestibular toxicity may be permanent. Monitor renal function, drug levels, and counsel patients about tinnitus and hearing changes.
  • Avoid prolonged courses — beyond 5–7 days, cumulative nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity risk rises substantially. For most systemic GNR infections, 5 days with a beta-lactam backbone is adequate; de-escalate to beta-lactam monotherapy once clinical response established.
  • Once-daily aminoglycosides should NOT be used in endocarditis, pregnancy, burns (>20% BSA), or ascites — altered volume of distribution invalidates standard nomograms. Use traditional dosing with individualized PK monitoring.
  • Amikacin retains activity when Gentamicin-resistant — different enzyme inactivation pathways. When susceptibilities show Gentamicin-resistance, Amikacin should be tested.
Drug Guide: Gentamicin Drug Guide: Amikacin IE Native card
Beta-Lactams — Selected Class Pearls
▼
  • Cefazolin is non-inferior to Nafcillin/Oxacillin for MSSA bacteremia — and has fewer adverse effects (hepatotoxicity, interstitial nephritis). IDSA guidelines now list Cefazolin as an acceptable alternative. Exception: CNS infections — Nafcillin preferred due to higher CSF penetration. PMID 29020263
  • Extended-infusion beta-lactam dosing optimizes T>MIC for resistant organisms — Pip-tazo 4.5 g over 4 hours q8h and Meropenem 2 g over 3 hours q8h maximize the pharmacodynamic target for elevated-MIC Pseudomonas and ESBL-producing organisms. Supported by Monte Carlo simulation data and clinical outcome studies. PMID 18281844
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam should NOT be used as definitive therapy for ESBL-producing Enterobacterales bacteremia — the MERINO trial demonstrated significantly higher 30-day mortality with Pip-Tazo vs. Meropenem for ceftriaxone-resistant E. coli and Klebsiella BSI (12.3% vs. 3.7%). Use a carbapenem. MERINO Trial PMID 30208454
  • Ceftriaxone 1× daily is pharmacodynamically appropriate for most indications — t½ ~8 hours; adequate T>MIC for susceptible organisms with once-daily dosing. 2 g q24h for meningitis and serious CNS infections (higher CSF concentrations).
  • Penicillin allergy cross-reactivity with cephalosporins is far lower than historical estimates — true cross-reactivity is ~1–2%, not 10%. Skin test or graded challenge is appropriate for patients with low-risk penicillin allergy history before avoiding all beta-lactams. Cephalosporins are not contraindicated in patients with a reported PCN allergy without documented anaphylaxis. PMID 25484630
  • Imipenem has the highest seizure risk among carbapenems — especially at high doses or in CNS disease / renal impairment. Meropenem is preferred for meningitis and CNS infections. Ertapenem lacks Pseudomonas coverage — do not use when Pseudomonas is a concern.

Tetracyclines / Glycylcyclines
▼
  • Doxycycline is the drug of choice for all tick-borne rickettsial diseases — including in children (AAP and CDC guidance supports Doxycycline even in children <8 years for RMSF when the diagnosis is suspected, due to risk of fatal delay). Do not substitute with other agents for RMSF — mortality rises sharply with delayed or incorrect therapy. CDC / AAP PMID 16964828
  • Doxycycline has high oral bioavailability (~93%) — strong IV-to-PO switch candidate. IV and PO doxycycline are clinically interchangeable in patients with intact GI absorption.
  • Minocycline > Doxycycline for Acinetobacter baumannii — Minocycline retains activity against strains resistant to Doxycycline; different efflux pump susceptibility profile. Consider for MDR Acinetobacter as part of combination regimen. PMID 26316527
  • Tigecycline: once-daily high-dose regimens are preferred — standard dosing (50 mg q12h) results in low serum concentrations. High-dose (100 mg q12h or 200 mg once daily) achieves better PK targets for serious infections. Do not use for bacteremia or UTI — serum and urine concentrations are inadequate. PMID 25733600
  • Tetracyclines are bacteriostatic — not for endocarditis monotherapy or other infections requiring bactericidal activity. Exception: Doxycycline for Brucella, rickettsial disease, atypical pneumonia where bacteriostatic activity is clinically effective.
  • Avoid dairy / divalent cations within 2 hours of oral dose — chelation reduces absorption for oral Doxycycline by up to 50%. Minocycline is less affected by food.

Macrolides / Azalides
▼
  • Azithromycin carries QTc prolongation risk — particularly relevant in patients with underlying cardiac disease, electrolyte abnormalities, or concurrent QTc-prolonging drugs. NEJM 2012 cardiovascular mortality signal in high-risk populations. Consider Doxycycline as alternative for atypical CAP in cardiac-risk patients. NEJM 2012 PMID 22475600
  • Azithromycin is NOT the preferred monotherapy for severe Legionella — Fluoroquinolones (Levofloxacin 750 mg) are preferred for ICU-level Legionnaire's disease due to superior intracellular killing. Azithromycin is appropriate for mild-moderate atypical CAP. IDSA/ATS 2019
  • High macrolide resistance rates in S. pneumoniae in many regions — check local antibiogram; macrolide monotherapy for CAP is inappropriate in areas with >25% macrolide-resistant pneumococcal rates. Use in combination with a beta-lactam or switch to a respiratory fluoroquinolone. IDSA/ATS 2019
  • Clarithromycin is a potent CYP3A4 inhibitor — significant drug interactions with statins, warfarin, cyclosporine, and many other agents. Prefer Azithromycin (minimal CYP3A4 effect) when interaction risk is a concern.
  • Azithromycin tissue concentrations greatly exceed serum levels — 3-day Z-pack achieves 5-day intracellular tissue concentrations. Serum levels do not reflect tissue PK. Drug persists in alveolar macrophages for days after last dose.

TMP-SMX (Trimethoprim-Sulfamethoxazole)
▼
  • Oral TMP-SMX achieves near-identical serum levels to IV — bioavailability ~90–100%. Excellent IV-to-PO switch candidate for MRSA SSTI, PCP, Nocardia, and susceptible UTI/pyelonephritis. Bone and CNS penetration are excellent. PMID 1849646
  • High-dose TMP-SMX is first-line for all severities of PCP — 15–20 mg/kg/day (TMP component). No agent has demonstrated superiority; alternatives (Primaquine-Clindamycin, Atovaquone, Pentamidine) carry higher failure/toxicity rates. DHHS OI Guidelines
  • False elevation of serum creatinine without true AKI — TMP inhibits tubular secretion of creatinine (competes with creatinine for organic cation transporter), raising SCr by 0.1–0.3 mg/dL without reducing GFR. Do not reflexively hold TMP-SMX for mild SCr elevation unless trend continues.
  • Hyperkalemia risk, especially in the elderly and CKD — TMP acts as a potassium-sparing diuretic (similar to amiloride), blocking sodium channels in the distal nephron. Monitor K⁺, especially with ACE inhibitors, ARBs, or spironolactone. PMID 25416274
  • Folate supplementation does not overcome TMP-SMX mechanism — TMP blocks bacterial DHFR; human cells use dietary folate directly. Supplemental folate does NOT reverse antimicrobial efficacy. Leucovorin rescue is used to reduce hematologic toxicity in high-risk patients (HIV, myelosuppression) — it does not diminish antibacterial activity.

Metronidazole
▼
  • Metronidazole is no longer first-line for any severity of CDI — IDSA 2021 guidelines replaced Metronidazole with Vancomycin PO or Fidaxomicin as preferred therapy for all severities. Metronidazole is retained for fulminant CDI only as an adjunct (IV) when enteric access is limited. IDSA CDI 2021 PMID 34519664
  • Excellent CNS penetration — achieves therapeutic concentrations in brain abscess; essential component of brain abscess empiric regimens when anaerobes are suspected.
  • Peripheral neuropathy with prolonged courses — cumulative exposure risk, particularly >4 weeks. Monitor for paresthesias. Usually reversible but may be prolonged.
  • Disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol — counsel all patients to strictly avoid alcohol and alcohol-containing products (including mouthwash) during and for 48 hours after therapy.
  • IV and PO Metronidazole are bioequivalent — oral bioavailability ~100%. Switch IV to PO as soon as GI tract is functional; no pharmacokinetic penalty.

Antifungals — Selected Pearls
▼
  • Echinocandins are first-line for candidemia in most adults — superior early fungicidal activity vs. azoles; activity against fluconazole-resistant Candida species. Micafungin, Caspofungin, Anidulafungin are options. Step-down to Fluconazole when susceptibilities confirmed and patient clinically stable. IDSA Candida 2016
  • Remove all intravascular catheters in candidemia — central lines are a persistent source; failure to remove is associated with higher mortality. Replace after repeat blood cultures clear.
  • Ophthalmology exam is mandatory in candidemia — Candida endophthalmitis occurs in ~10–15% of candidemia and may be asymptomatic. Funduscopic exam by ophthalmology within 1 week of diagnosis.
  • Voriconazole has complex PK with nonlinear saturable hepatic metabolism — CYP2C19 polymorphisms cause wide interpatient variability. Therapeutic drug monitoring is essential; target trough 1–5.5 mcg/mL. Below 1 = likely subtherapeutic; above 5.5 = neurotoxicity risk. IDSA Aspergillus 2016
  • Amphotericin B — liposomal formulation preferred for most indications — dramatically lower nephrotoxicity vs. conventional Ampho B deoxycholate, equivalent efficacy. Use conventional formulation only when cost is prohibitive and careful monitoring is feasible.
  • Azoles are potent CYP inhibitors — drug interaction minefield — Fluconazole (CYP2C9, 3A4) and Voriconazole/Posaconazole (3A4, 2C19) interact with tacrolimus, cyclosporine, warfarin, statins, many chemotherapy agents. Review all medications before prescribing.

Section B · By Syndrome
Apparent Antibiotic Failure — Framework
▼
  • Most "antibiotic failures" are not antibiotic failures — before changing antibiotics, systematically rule out: undrained source (abscess, empyema, infected hardware), drug fever, non-infectious fever mimics, wrong diagnosis, inadequate source control, or superinfection.
  • Drug fever can mimic treatment failure — occurs most commonly 7–10 days into a course; any antibiotic can cause it. Suspect if patient appears well despite fever, eosinophilia is present, and cultures remain negative. Stopping the offending drug results in defervescence within 48–72h.
  • Persistent fever after 72h of appropriate antibiotics — reassess: Is the diagnosis correct? Is there a drainable source? Is the organism susceptible? Are concentrations adequate at the site of infection? Is there a complication (septic thrombophlebitis, endocarditis, metastatic foci)?
  • Inadequate source control is the most common cause of true antibiotic failure in complicated infections — abscess, infected prosthetic material, obstructed biliary tract, empyema, and infected hardware will not respond to antibiotics alone regardless of in vitro susceptibility.
Bacteremia / Bloodstream Infection
▼
  • All S. aureus bacteremia requires echocardiography — TEE is strongly preferred over TTE; superior sensitivity for vegetations, perivalvular abscess. Endocarditis cannot be excluded by TTE alone. IDSA S. aureus PMID 26373316
  • CoNS bacteremia: first evaluate for catheter source — a single blood culture positive for CoNS is usually contamination. Two separate positive cultures, especially with an indwelling catheter, increases probability of true BSI. Remove catheter when possible.
  • Gram-negative BSI: look for urinary, biliary, or GI source first — source localization drives both antibiotic selection and source control strategy.
  • Repeat blood cultures at 48–72h for all S. aureus BSI — persistent bacteremia beyond 72h signals a complication: endocarditis, septic thrombophlebitis, metastatic foci, or inadequate source control. Duration of therapy is measured from first negative blood culture.

Fever — Clinical Diagnostic Pearls
▼
  • Relative bradycardia (Faget's sign) narrows the differential — fever without the expected compensatory tachycardia (pulse-temperature dissociation) is classically associated with: Legionella, Salmonella typhi, Brucella, Psittacosis, viral infections (dengue, yellow fever), and drug fever. An infectious cause with expected tachycardia makes these less likely.
  • Fever pattern has diagnostic value when consistently recorded — quotidian (daily spike): visceral leishmaniasis, miliary TB, juvenile idiopathic arthritis; tertian/quartan (every 48–72h): malaria; saddle-back (biphasic): dengue, Colorado tick fever, yellow fever.
  • Legionnaires' disease: six characteristic clinical predictors (Cunha criteria) — high fever (>39°C), relative bradycardia, diarrhea, CNS manifestations, elevated LDH, and low sodium. Cluster of these features should prompt urine Legionella antigen and empiric coverage.
  • Highly elevated serum ferritin (>10,000 ng/mL) is a key clue for specific diagnoses — adult-onset Still's disease, hemophagocytic lymphohistiocytosis (HLH), miliary TB, and visceral leishmaniasis. Extreme hyperferritinemia in the right clinical context should prompt workup for these entities, not broader empiric antibiotics.
